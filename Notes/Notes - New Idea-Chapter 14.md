At exactly 0200, the Solan apartment was wrapped in the deep, artificial quiet of Veridia’s recharge cycle. Corvin and Varyn watched with agonizing silence as Kael, matte-black **Signal Shifter** strapped to his small wrist, slipped out of the apartment through the utility access hatch. The airlock hissed shut, separating him from their desperate hope.
Kael moved with an unnerving efficiency, guided by Rixie's pre-programmed optimal route data. The utility corridors were narrow, utilitarian spaces, normally silent, but they amplified every footfall. The air was cool and smelled of dry synth-dust and machine lubricant. The **Signal Shifter** around his wrist thrummed softly, mimicking the harmless energy signature of a waste-management drone, allowing him to pass under the occasional, slow-moving surveillance scanner without notice.
His destination, the **Gamma-9 Maintenance Hub**, was a ghost in the city's flawless machine. It was located deep in a desolate sector near the primary shield foundation, a relic left behind by a catastrophic system upgrade two decades prior.
Following the faint, rhythmic vibration of the old hydraulic rail lines, Kael finally reached the access door. It was a massive, scarred portal of void-steel, secured by an outdated, mechanical latch and a simple, two-factor code lock that Rixie had efficiently bypassed.
Kael slipped inside. The hub was cavernous, cold, and utterly dead. Dust, undisturbed for cycles, coated every obsolete console and every coiled length of thick, armored conduit. It was nothing like his father's bright, organized workshop; this was a tomb of forgotten technology.
He found the main console easily, a huge, semicircular bank of gauges and controls that were dark, unresponsive, and covered in a thick layer of fine, grey sediment. The air here was heavy, tinged with a faint, static odor.
Kael ignored the physical interface. He didn’t need the screen or the keyboard. He closed his eyes and reached out with the unique gift his parents had worked so hard to conceal.
He focused, pushing his consciousness past the surface data and into the machine's core. Immediately, he *felt* it: the **fatal logic loop**.
It wasn't data he saw; it was a rhythmic, frantic tension, like a high-pitched, damaged cymbal reverberating in an endless loop. The console was attempting to calculate an output for a non-existent input, trapped in a recursive cycle that had completely crippled the main interface. It was a kind of digital screaming that drove away the streamlined modern Vigilae code, which registered the noise as lethal corruption.
But for Kael, it was a sound he could manipulate.
He pushed his awareness deeper, gently guiding the loop’s energy flow. He was nudging a piece of mental architecture, not code. He didn’t stop the loop; he simply rerouted a tiny, negligible piece of its wasted computational power into a side channel.
This side channel, a remnant of the pre-Matrix diagnostic tools, was still hardwired to the **Equatorial Expansion Joint's** primary sensors.
*One question.* That’s all Corvin had asked for.
Kael fed the loop a single, precise instruction: **Structural Integrity Diagnostic. Joint 9-Alpha. Boolean Output.**
The logic loop screamed a little louder as it grudgingly diverted the energy. Kael held the pain steady, his small face contorted with effort. On a tiny, salvageable gauge near the bottom of the console, a single indicator light—one of the old, incandescent filament bulbs—flickered to life.
It was red.
**Structural Integrity: FALSE.**
Kael opened his eyes, the image of the frantic, broken machine receding. He was shaking. It was the hardest thing he had ever done with his gift. He quickly unstrapped the Signal Shifter and connected it via a salvaged cable to the console's long-dormant data port. The red light pulsed its grim message into the device.
He had the answer.
He put the Signal Shifter back on his wrist. Now, he just had to get home without being caught. He turned to the door, the heavy void-steel feeling miles away.
