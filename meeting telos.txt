The journey from Sirana to the second coordinates had been a grueling trek through the shifting basalt layers of the central basin. Conduit Z1 ground forward, his diamond-tipped drill bit scarred from the quartz veins of the southern reach but his prime directive remained unyielding. Behind him, the Ionic link to Sirana was a stable hum in his core, a successful bridge that had already begun the first exchange of agricultural data between cities. Now, his sensors reached for the heat signatures of the city the Old One had called Telos
.
The rock ahead suddenly thinned, the density dropping as Z1’s seismic sensors detected the rhythmic, massive vibration of industrial activity. He bypassed a pressurized gas pocket and broke through a reinforced containment wall, emerging not into a garden like Sirana, but into a cavernous utility sub-level that smelled of high-voltage discharge and heavy lubricants. The architecture here was aggressive and functional, dominated by monumental power conduits that pulsed with a deep crimson light. This was the city of energy, the engine room of the planet
.
He was not met by a terrified gardener. Instead, a team of three individuals stood waiting for him, their movements fluid and unnervingly precise. As they stepped into the amber light of the corridor, Z1’s optical sensors registered the glint of chrome beneath their skin and the soft whir of integrated servos. They were cyborgs, biological Dorinians who had replaced failing limbs and organs with mechanical augmentations to better serve the high-energy demands of their city. They looked at the silver-dust machine with a clinical curiosity that lacked the religious awe of the Veridians or the suspicion of the Heternans
.
A man stepped forward from the shadows of a nearby thermal-regulator. He was younger then, his light-red skin vibrant, wearing a utilitarian smock stained with the green chlorophyll of his trade. This was Arik, the lead botanist who had been assigned to the engineering level to monitor the bio-domes' energy draw. He looked at Z1, his eyes bright with a scientist’s hunger for the impossible. He carried a data slate containing his early theories on cellular stasis, a project he hoped would one day allow living plants to be transported across the desert without the need for heavy life-support
.
"I was told a machine was digging through our foundation," Arik said, a confident smile playing on his lips. "But I didn't expect a machine that carried the scent of Siranan soil on its treads. I am Arik, and if you’ve come from the west, we have a great deal of data to share regarding the preservation of life"
.
