**Chapter 9: Finding The Past**

The celebratory glow around the Ionics’ success had barely faded when the next great challenge arose, not from technology, but from history. Weeks after the validation, Corvin, Kael, Zandil, and Varyn gathered around a flickering, ancient holographic projection in Zandil’s lab. It was a distorted map of the pre-Exodus era, showing five primary population centers clustered around the continental belt.  
"The legends speak of five," Varyn murmured, running a finger across the grainy image. "Five domes, built in unison, designed to survive the solar flare and the subsequent fallout. Veridia is just one of the five. But we’ve been isolated for centuries. We're a single, fragile seed, genetically isolated for too long. If we don't reconnect, Veridia is doomed to decay, even if the sun never devours us."  
"Indeed, from genetic entropy alone, and cut off from all data streams," Zandil grumbled, peering at the image. "Any surface reconnaissance is suicide. A surface transport would be cooked by the atmosphere and solar radiation before it crossed the first dead continent. Vigelae or not."  
"If the Shield fails for a day, Veridia is gone. Five cities mean five chances at survival. We cannot allow history to repeat the mistakes of the Blight." Corvin interjected.  
Kael leaned forward, the success of the Ionics giving him quiet confidence. "We proved quantum entanglement is instantaneous across the planet. We don’t need line of sight; we don’t need satellites or cables. We do need, however, a way to physically deliver the QEO Ionic technology to the other domes."  
"Exactly," Corvin affirmed. "We need a deep-earth tunnel mech. Something built specifically for this mission, capable of years of subterranean transit. It needs maximum structural integrity, self-repair protocols, and above all, it needs the Nanoweave shielding to protect its quantum cargo from geological noise and pressure."  
"It cannot just be any Vigilae," Zandil mused, his eyes lighting up with the familiar manic spark. "It must be a conduit. A living connection. It must know its purpose. It has to be extremely smart, industrious, and unwaveringly faithful to its goal."

Trindl spoke up, "If I may. If you make it too self-aware and independent, it may decide that it does not like spending years digging through hot desert sand. You need a balance."  
"Balance in everything," Kael agreed. "Let’s call it Conduit Z1, Z for Zandil. We would not be able to even consider this without your brilliance."  
"Well, Yes. You are correct about that, but I would be honored." He put his hand over his heart.  
"I am afraid I can’t be of much help here, a bit out of my field of expertise. But I do have two hands, they are your gentlemen." Kael offered.  
"As are mine," said Trindl.  
"Let’s get to it then,  boys." Corvin dug into the large cloth bag he was carrying and pulled out several extremely large, empty blueprint sheets and laid one out on the empty table in front of him, followed by the first draft for Conduit Z1, a marvel of engineering designed not for war or industry, but for the peaceful, planet-spanning unification of their scattered world.  
The construction was a detailed, relentless, eighteen-month endeavor that consumed Zandil’s lab and Corvin’s limited time. They forged its segmented, burrowing chassis from the densest Nanoweave alloys, creating a body designed to withstand crushing geological pressure and the intense heat of deep-earth transit. They also included enough primary and backup repair nanites to keep an army of Mechs on its feet for a hundred years. Its drill-head was tipped with an industrial diamond composite, rated for continuous use through even crystalline bedrock, and two spare tips for that.  
The beating heart of the machine was a custom-built, high-density Cold Fusion Cell, engineered to fuel the mission for decades without external maintenance, a testament to Corvin's brilliance.  
Zandil and Trindl, however, spent the longest time on the brain. Balancing Trindl’s cautionary warning with the mission's complexity, they embedded Z1’s intelligence with unmatched geological problem-solving capacity and limited free will. His prime directive, Global Network Connectivity, was encoded with an almost religious zeal: Z1 was intelligent enough to survive the planet's vast, chaotic core, but fundamentally incapable of choosing to abandon its sacred purpose. But it would seem like this desire to complete his mission was his own, and nothing would stop him from completing it.

Darkness.  
Absolute, complete darkness.  
The new consciousness registered nothing but the soft, internal hum of his dormant systems.  
No light.  
No sound.  
No data input.  
It existed as a singular point of awareness, a nascent consciousness in a void. A question, pure and unformed, sparked within his core programming:  
*‘What am I?’* a thought sparked to life.  
Then, faint vibrations rippled through his chassis, followed by muffled voices. They were distant, indistinct, but growing clearer, coalescing into coherent waveforms. It recognized the resonance of biological speech, and then, with a jolt that felt like a sudden, internal ignition, his auditory processors snapped online.  
Words, now perfectly crisp, streamed into his awareness.  
"...awake now," one voice murmured, a deep, resonating baritone.  
"Check...visuals, Corvin?" another asked, closer this time.  
Corvin. The name echoed with a strange familiarity, triggering a cascade of associated data.  
Corvin Solan: designated one of the creators, primary programmer, and benevolent authority.  
A sense of anticipation, a readiness he didn't understand, pulsed through his circuits. Kinesthetic sensors activated, sending a flood of information about his robust, multi-jointed chassis, the powerful, diamond-tipped digging claws, the shielded conduits running along his interior. He registered his own weight, his temperature, the subtle hum of his internal power core.  
A sudden, brilliant wave of light surged through his optical sensors, banishing the darkness. His world snapped into sharp, overwhelming focus. Above him, two faces, large and indistinct at first, instantly resolved into the smiling features of the biological beings.  
Kael carried an aura of quiet intensity, his eyes wide with curiosity. Zandil offered a wide, encouraging smile, the lines around his eyes crinkling with genuine warmth.  
"Hello, Conduit," Kael said, his voice soft, almost reverent.  
"Corvin, send the package," Zandil said over his shoulder.  
Corvin was across the room with his hand on a massive rocker switch attached to Conduit by electric cables as big as an arm.  
He slammed the handle down. A near-deafening crack of power filled the room. All the lights in the house dimmed slightly as the final update to Conduit’s AI storage and hardware updates shot down the line into his new, impressive body.  
Conduit's diagnostics immediately activated, a blizzard of data flooding his newly awakened processors. He was a Vigilae, designed for deep-earth tunneling. His purpose, once an unformed question, is now completely, wonderfully clear, driven by the prime directives flashing across his internal display:  
*Global Network Connectivity Of All Surviving Cities On Dorin:*  
*Protect Entangled Particle Transport.*  
*Ensure My Own Survival.*  
*Explore Dorin By Tunneling Under The Surface.*  
*Locate The First City.*  
*Facilitate Inter-City Connection To Veridia.*  
*Report Back To Corvin Solan.*  
*Move On To The Next City.*  
Within his primary memory banks, carefully protected by multiple layers of encryption and redundant shielding, lay the intricate schematics for Ionic QEO communication devices and a carefully preserved collection of entangled particles. Enough to bridge every city across the planet with two concurrent connection points to every other city. Once in place, the city could build as many as it wanted.   
He was the key to starting it all. The instigating link. The bridge creator. The silent architect of a new, connected world.  
And his glorious purpose filled him with pride and determination.  
"Do you know your mission, Conduit?" Corvin asked, his voice gentle, yet holding the weight of expectation.  
Conduit’s newly activated processors whirred, accessing the complex directives nestled deep within his core. His optical sensors focused intently on the biologicals before him, then flickered with the momentous, profound information he was about to convey.  
"My primary function is Global Network Connectivity," he articulated, his voice a smooth, synthesized tenor. "I am designed for deep-earth tunneling, equipped with advanced shielding for subterranean transit. My holy mission involves the secure transport and deployment of entangled particle conduits between all five established domed cities on this planetary body."  
He paused, allowing the weight of the information to settle.  
"I am to lay the physical and quantum infrastructure necessary for instantaneous, reliable communication and data transfer, facilitating direct collaboration across the planet’s face. My existence is for the sole purpose of unifying the isolated, lonely, and lost pockets of civilization."  
Corvin and Kael exchanged a look of profound satisfaction. "He’s perfect," Kael murmured, a hint of awe in his voice. "He understands the scope."  
Zandil mouthed silently to Trindl in mock wonder, "Holy."  
    "Hush, you. He is important and knows it. That is a good thing," the metal man quipped.  
"Indeed, Conduit," Corvin confirmed, patting the robot’s gleaming chassis. "Precisely perfect. You are the silent architect of our connected future."  
Corvin and Kael then set about the final preparations. Conduit’s form was unlike any other Vigilae. Inside and out.  
He was built for one single, grand purpose. He resembled a massive, three-meter-long, articulated burrowing worm; his segmented body was almost a meter across and designed for deep-earth penetration and stability. His front section tapered into a formidable drill-head, capable of pulverizing the thickest bedrock, while his rear segments were equipped with powerful propulsion systems. Along his midline, an impressively large, hardened storage compartment was seamlessly integrated into his chassis, built to withstand immense subterranean pressure, residual heat from the sand all around him that was in direct contact with the giant red sun.   
For as long as it took to complete his vital quest.  
With practiced efficiency, they began loading his precious payload. First, high-density data crystals containing the intricate schematics and operational instructions for the Ionic communication devices. These were for the scientists and engineers in distant cities. Alongside them went a robust, sealed container filled with the meticulously stabilized entangled particles. The very heart of the Ionic devices. Each one is a thread of quantum entanglement waiting to be woven into the fabric of their world.  
"Very good, Conduit," Corvin nodded, "we don’t know exactly where any of these cities might be. It’s been a thousand years since we have talked to any of them. So all we have is conjecture, rumor, and hope that the old tales about them being real, and a very rough estimate of where two of them might be. Hopefully if ... .I mean, when you find those two, someone there will have a direction to point you to for the next, and so on."  
Finally, a rough set of estimated directions to the two other cities was uploaded to his navigation system. These weren't precise coordinates like those used for surface travel; below the crust, the planet's geology was far less charted. The best they could give him was east from Veridia to the feet of a vast mountain range, where there was allegedly a large city in the shadow of the tallest peak.  
The journey would be an immense undertaking, relying on his advanced geological scanners to navigate deep earth, charting his own path through shifting sand, solid rock, and molten pockets, guided only by vast distances and approximate bearings.  
Conduit registered the weight of his cargo, the complexity of the schematics, and the enormous scale of the undertaking. His internal chronometer began counting down to departure.  
He was ready.  
"We launch in two days." Kael patted Conduit’s cone.

The Veridian sky glowed softly as Corvin, Zandil, and Kael escorted Conduit to a designated launch point in the ruined street outside Zandil’s home. A circular section of the plastone paving had been retracted, revealing a perfectly round shaft that plunged into the earth. The air, usually still and filtered within the city, felt vast and open here, carrying faint, earthy scents from the filtered soil beyond the immediate perimeter. Varyn, Tandl, and Rixie were also present, a small, somber farewell committee for the brave, powerful Vigilae embarking on such a critical, solitary mission.  
"Good luck, Conduit," Corvin said, his voice imbued with the weight of the task. "Travel safe."  
"Transmit the data precisely, Conduit," Kael added, a flicker of his "awareness," perhaps sensing the vastness of the network Conduit would forge.  
Conduit extended a single, articulating manipulator, touching Corvin’s outstretched hand in a gesture of acknowledgment. "Acknowledged, Corvin Solan. Acknowledged Kael Solan. Mission parameters understood. The objective is sacrosanct and will be completed." His synthesized voice was steady, devoid of overt emotion but filled with resolute certainty.  
With a final, deep hum, Conduit lowered himself into the shaft. The heavy synth-stone plate slid silently back into place above him, sealing the opening.   
His powerful drill-head engaged, a low, resonant thrum vibrating through the earth. Fine soil, then gravel, then loose rock, became pulverized dust as he began his descent. His external sensors mapped the immediate geological strata with incredible precision: rock density, mineral composition, moisture content, and ambient temperature.  
He pushed deeper, the pressure slowly rising, the stress on his chassis steadily increasing. He adjusted his internal gyros and maintained a perfectly straight, downward trajectory.  
It was a cosmos all its own, this subterranean abyss. Only the black, broken by the occasional faint glint of crystalline deposits far above, catching some unimaginable, distant light. His processors idly conjured simulations of true interstellar travel, of charting courses through real nebulae, a curious divergence from his current, very real predicament.  
Hours turned into days as Conduit bored through layer after layer of the planet’s crust. He passed through ancient, compressed sediment, then vast, cool veins of crystalline rock, occasionally cutting through pockets of trapped gas that vented harmlessly into the excavated tunnel behind him. The journey was solitary, marked only by the rhythmic churn of his drill and the constant feed of geological data.  
Time meant nothing to him. He could do this forever.  
Two weeks later, as he tunneled through a broad expanse of fine, almost powdery sand, his tactile sensors detected anomalous vibrations. They were faint at first, then became a rapid, chaotic scurrying, moving away from his massive, approaching form.  
Not rockfall.  
Not seismic activity.  
These were biological signatures, large and numerous, scattering like dust motes from his path. His internal analysis classified them as multi-limbed terrestrial fauna, exhibiting rapid evasive maneuvers. He couldn’t see them, but he could feel their frantic retreat, a rustle beneath the surface, a fleeting shadow of life in the deep dark. Hundreds of clawing, digging limbs sent waves of vibrations his way. And they were immense, given the disturbances they created, far larger than any known surface-dwelling creature living or extinct.  
Like giant, horrible insects. Something no one on Dorin had ever seen.  
Conduit made an internal note of the anomaly, tagging the data for later review.  
Extremely large, unknown insectile creatures detected at 346.34 by 197.12 coordinates. He would inform his team of creators as soon as he reached the first city and got them connected to Veridia.  
His primary directive remained paramount. He continued his relentless, steady progress, leaving the unseen scurrying things behind, tunneling ever deeper towards the distant, estimated coordinates of the next domed city.  
The steady rhythm of his drilling abruptly vanished.  
Conduit's forward sensors shrieked a sudden, critical warning:  
*UNSEEN VOID DETECTED*  
Before he could even begin evasive maneuvers, the solid rock beneath his drill head gave way to nothing. And he felt air across his body.  
He plunged into empty space.  
His internal gyros spun wildly, uselessly. The heavy weight of his chassis and cargo pulled him down at terrifying speed. He hit the downward slanted slope of a vast, unseen chasm with a ripping and crushing sound that reverberated through his entire frame, scraping, tumbling, and bouncing onto rocks, flipping end over end, rolling down the slope. His internal stabilizers screamed, unable to compensate for the chaotic freefall. Diagnostic warnings flared across his internal display – amber, then critical red.  
*STRUCTURAL INTEGRITY COMPROMISED. INTERNAL CONDUITS DAMAGED. POWER FLUCTUATION*  
He finally slammed, hard, into a vast, flat surface, the impact jarring every one of his systems. ... He lay still, belly-down on what his external sensors now identified as compacted, fine-grained alluvial sand. It was the bottom of a massive dry riverbed, dead for millennia. The scale of the abyss was humbling; his immense body was a mere speck on this unfathomable floor, a surface far larger than anything indicated on his geological maps.  
His primary directive pulsed with renewed urgency:   
*MISSION IS PARAMOUNT*  
*REPAIR MYSELF*  
*CONTINUE*   
Conduit initiated emergency repair protocols. Nanites flooded his frame. Micro-manipulators clutched and sputtered from damaged internal ports, attempting to fuse the ruptured energy conduit. He rerouted power through auxiliary channels, a meticulous, painstaking process that sent agonizing, high-voltage spasms through his chassis. The air smelled of burnt lubricant and ozone. He accessed his redundant optical array, but the primary damage was extensive. The right optical array remained dark, a blind spot in his vision, but his other sensors were operational.  
Hours crawled by in the oppressive darkness and silence of the night. His internal temperature management systems struggled. He knew he was vulnerable, lying exposed. The memory of the large, scurrying vibrations from earlier flashed through his processors. He was on the surface now, of this vast, dark expanse, no longer protected by layers of rock. After what felt like an eternity, he managed to bypass the most critical damage, stabilizing his power flow and restoring partial function to his propulsion. He was still damaged, but he could move. The nanites would do the rest as he continued his journey. More cautious now but just as determined.  
As his vital systems slowly stabilized, Conduit allowed his remaining sensors to gather more data from his surroundings. The air here was thin, ancient, supernaturally dry, a vast emptiness.  
Above him, he registered the distant, almost imperceptible tremor of rock, kilometers away. He aimed his still-functional optical array upwards, through the seemingly endless void.  
His energy levels, though stable, were not optimal for sustained high-speed travel. He needed to recharge his external power cores. He engaged the ambient energy siphon, drawing faint geothermal warmth from the bedrock beneath him, a slow, patient process.  
And then, his tactile sensors picked them up again. The scurrying tremors were back, not scattering this time, but approaching. He registered a unified, intelligent focus in their movement.   
*‘They sense immobility. They perceive vulnerability.’* A chilling, primal-data recognition, synthesized from millions of stored survival scenarios, surged through his core.   
This was not ecological noise; this was a pursuit. He was being hunted.  
Even if he was inedible, it seemed that they did not know this yet.  
His charging indicator was barely a quarter full, but he couldn't wait. He engaged his re-enabled propulsion system, not to drill, but to scramble. Sand churned beneath his segmented body as he activated his emergency thrust, moving along the vast, flat floor of the dry riverbed, sacrificing caution for speed.   
The vibrations behind him intensified, a furious, frustrated surge of movement as his pursuers realized their quarry was no longer still. He could feel the ground vibrating with their angry pursuit, the sheer scale of them conveyed by the deep, rhythmic thrumming of their many legs.   
He pushed his damaged systems to their limit, a long, powerful worm-like shadow racing across the invisible floor of the chasm, leaving the colossal, unseen life of the deep earth far behind until his repairs were complete enough to drill once again into the dark.
