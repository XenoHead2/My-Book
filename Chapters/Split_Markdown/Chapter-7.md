**Chapter 7 \- Non-Local Non-Zero**

Hours earlier, the hum of the climate control was the only sound in the Solan-Vira apartment as Corvin and Varyn waited. They did not speak; they merely watched the utility access hatch.

When Kael finally slipped back through, silent as a ghost, Varyn was instantly by his side. She checked his breathing, his pulse, her worry momentarily eclipsing her training.

"I’m fine, Mom," Kael whispered, his voice thin with exhaustion. He immediately unstrapped the matte-black Signal Shifter and handed it to Corvin. "It’s false, Dad. Structural Integrity: FALSE."

Corvin took the device, his hands trembling slightly as he plugged it into his secure terminal. Varyn watched the screen, her heart in her throat. The screen displayed the raw data feed, a string of complex figures, ending with the single, damning Boolean Output: 0\.

"The Equatorial Joint," Corvin breathed, reading the coordinates. "The stress point. It’s degrading."

Varyn sank onto the couch, running a hand through her hair. "A crack. A hairline fracture. It’s microscopic, but it’s there. The city has a breach."

"It’s worse than a breach, Varyn," Corvin said, his voice flat with horror. "If it’s degrading, it means the Nanoweave is compromised. The shield is failing at its most critical point. We have to monitor the growth of that crack."

"But Kael just proved we can’t," Varyn countered desperately. "Any scan from the main network will be instantly flagged by the Vigilae Sentinels. They’ll swarm the area."

Corvin stared at the zero on the screen, a number that represented the death of their city. "We need eyes on that crack, Varyn, with continuous monitoring of the quantum state of the structure around it. But we can’t use light, energy, or radio. It would expose us."

They worked in tense silence for the next few hours, attempting to devise a way to run a silent, invisible diagnostic. They failed. Every possible solution required a resource or authority they did not possess, or a system that the Vigilae would immediately trace.

The sun had long since risen and set again when the family finally collapsed into the living area, the weight of their secret pressing down on them.

The Temple of Physics dominated Veridia's intellectual sector skyline. Its crystalline spire, catching the city's filtered sunlight, pierced the domed ceiling, a monument to the collective brilliance housed within. Below, massive, polished weave-steel letters proudly proclaimed its name, a beacon for all who worshipped at the altar of scientific understanding. Within its hallowed walls resided Veridia's greatest minds, their work meticulously funded and rigorously peer reviewed, all focused on pushing the boundaries of microcosmic theories and the vast houses of physics.

Yet, in a pristine, silently humming waiting room on a high floor, the atmosphere was far from serene. Zandil, looking remarkably disheveled even for him, bounced his knee against a chair that seemed designed for maximum discomfort. His wild hair was, if possible, even more untamed than usual, and his eyes, usually blazing with scientific fervor, now held an impatient glint. Beside him, Trindl sat with perfect posture, his silver form unmoving, a picture of Vigilae's composure. Except for the almost imperceptible flicker in his optical sensors.

"How long is ‘a little while longer,’ Trindl?" Zandil muttered, his voice a low growl that vibrated with contained frustration. "The attendant has uttered that phrase precisely five times now. Does ‘little while’ have some variable meaning in formal institutions that I am not privy to?" He asked, as he buried his face in his dark red hands, his long, bent ear tips drooping over his knuckles.

Trindl’s resonant baritone was as calm as ever. "The average human perception of ‘a little while’ in a bureaucratic context can range from ten standard minutes to two standard hours, my love. Given the estimated importance of your proposed paradigm shift, the current delay is statistically within expected parameters." Tridl morphed his ferrofluid in a sympathetic grin.

"Parameters, bah\!" Zandil scoffed, running a hand through his already wild hair. "They’re probably trying to figure out what I could have to tell them. Doing all the info checks and background lookups, " he made a raspberry with his mouth and tongue, "Bah\! They won’t find anything. I am like a shadow\! A smart, scientific shadow with…uh…." he said, losing the analogy,"  never mind. I’m so bored."

Eventually, a soft buzzer chimed, and the attendant, with a practiced, almost weary smile, announced, "You may go in now."

Zandil, with a final impatient huff, strode towards the indicated door, Trindl gliding silently behind him. "Red-sun rot\!" He swore as he stood. "About time. I’m not getting any younger."

They entered a spacious, sterile chamber dominated by a long, wide table that hovered at least a meter above the polished floor. On the far side sat five figures, three men and two women, and one very tall Vigilae, their expressions a mixture of curiosity and thinly veiled condescension. They were adorned in elaborate robes, each sporting large sashes of variable color and size, embellished with numerous stones of different cuts and luminescence. These were clearly badges of rank and scientific achievement within the Temple.

Looking down at Zandil from his central position, the man with the largest sash and the most numerous, glittering stones spoke, his voice carrying a note of bored authority. "You may speak. What do you want from us?"

"Want from you?" Zandil snorted, his voice echoing slightly in the large room. "Nothing. I have something you will want\!" He straightened his back and puffed out his chest.

A ripple of amusement, bordering on outright laughter, spread across the faces at the table. The Vigilae chuckled dryly. "We need nothing from you, old man."

"I beg to differ, I possess a unique knowledge of something that you, sand-blast-it, and no one in this entire city, perhaps no one in the history of Dorin, has ever conceived\!" Zandil declared, his voice rising with passionate conviction. "I know how to create it. And, more importantly, why it works. Now," he challenged, gesturing towards their sashes with a sardonic grin, "wouldn’t you like to add this knowledge to your… sashes or something?"

The leader’s amusement vanished. He pressed a subtle control on the table, and instantly, a shimmering, opalescent film enveloped Zandil and Trindl, forming a silent, white bubble around them. Zandil saw the figures across the table begin to speak, their mouths moving, but their words were utterly lost within the sound-dampening sphere.

After a tense moment, the white film dissipated as suddenly as it had appeared. The leader’s gaze, now sharp and assessing, fixed on Zandil. "You have our attention," he stated, his tone devoid of its earlier levity. "What precisely do you know?"

Zandil launched into his explanation, his words tumbling out in a torrent of scientific jargon and barely contained excitement, describing his manipulation of photons, the creation of entangled pairs, and the instantaneous correlation he had observed.

The figures across the table listened, their initial amusement replaced by skepticism. Murmurs of "impossible," "heresy," and "delusional" rippled amongst them.

"Madness," the leader finally pronounced, shaking his head dismissively. "These are fanciful notions, unsupported by any established theory."

"But I can prove it\!" Zandil insisted, his voice tight with frustration. "I have a working, repeatable prototype in my lab\!"

After a brief, hushed discussion amongst themselves, the council leader addressed Zandil again, a pragmatic glint in his eye. "Very well. We will grant you the opportunity to demonstrate your… claims. We will dispatch a Vigilae Report Unit with you back to your laboratory. This unit will record everything it observes with its enhanced optic sensors across the entire electromagnetic spectrum. It cannot be fooled or tricked. If your assertions hold merit, the unit will report its findings."

A sleek, silver Vigilae Report Unit detached itself from a charging station in the corner of the room. Its chassis was a seamless, polished cone, tapering slightly toward the top, and it moved with a silent, frictionless glide. The cone’s surface shimmered with an array of internal light, highlighting small, opalescent display screens embedded in its sides. As it approached Zandil and Trindl, several thin, segmented observation arms extended from its base, each tipped with a different sensory apparatus, a spectroscopic lens, a thermal scanner, and an electromagnetic sensor, all radiating an aura of cold, objective observation.

Trindl whispered, "This is a very thorough investigator, my love. I hope you know what you are doing."

Back in Zandil’s chaotic basement lab, the Vigilae Report Unit meticulously documented every aspect of Zandil’s demonstration. It recorded the laser firing into the bismuth-silicate crystal, the subtle energy fluctuations, and the readings from Zandil’s array of salvaged sensors. When Zandil separated the entangled photons and demonstrably influenced the state of one, causing an instantaneous correlated change in the other, the Vigilae unit’s optical sensors glowed with enhanced intensity. Its internal processors whirred as it analyzed the data.

Without uttering a single word, the Vigilae Report Unit concluded its observation, turned with precise movements, and glided back towards the Temple of Physics to deliver its irrefutable, multi-spectral record.

Zandil turned to Trindl, put his arm around him, and said, "That’ll show those know-nothing, know-it-all council ignoramuses."

"Yes, indeed, my love. They will be shown," and kissed him on the cheek.

Later that evening, the soft, ambient light of the simulated fireplace flickered across the polished void-steel walls. Kael, Corvin, and Varyn were gathered in the living area, the earlier tension of the data confirmation still thick in the air. A large, wall-mounted Vidsplay shimmered with the latest city-wide Info-Cast – Veridia’s nightly broadcast of events, news, updates, and lifestyle pieces projected as a dynamic, holographic display.

The current segment featured a serious-looking official in Temple of Physics robes standing before the crystalline spire. Behind him, holographic diagrams of light beams splitting and strange, interconnected particles pulsed in the air.

"...and thus, the Temple of Physics has confirmed an extraordinary, albeit controversial, discovery by an independent researcher," he held up a data crystal sheet and squinted at it. "Zandil Aethel." the official intoned, his voice carrying an air of cautious astonishment. "Preliminary findings suggest a previously unknown phenomenon involving subatomic particles, which appear to exhibit an instantaneous, correlated relationship across spatial distances. This has been tentatively termed ‘quantum entanglement’ by the discovering scientist. However, further research is being allocated to understand the full implications of this potentially paradigm-shifting discovery…"

Kael watched the holographic display, his brow furrowed in thought. Beside him, Varyn exchanged a skeptical glance with Corvin.

"Quantum entanglement," Varyn murmured. "Instantaneous correlation… It sounds… made up."

Corvin nodded slowly, the grim data from the Equatorial Joint burning in his mind. "Yes, and costly. The energy requirements alone for such a connection across any significant distance would be astronomical."

But Kael’s attention was no longer on the official’s cautious pronouncements. His eyes were wide with a sudden flash of insight.

‘If the state of a particle could be instantaneously linked and transmitted, could anything else be affected? Or even transmitted?’ His mind began to race, the gears of his intellect turning rapidly. ‘If not just the state or the charge, could data, could information itself be moved in this way? Instantly?’

Kael’s world began to slip out of focus, and the light that engulfed his mind from almost externally projected ideas surged. Like his ability to feel the state of electronics and circuitry, he was filled with a sense that seemed to originate from outside, pushing its way into him, but still as much a part of him as his own skin. This sudden flash of knowledge, of insight, and awareness of possibilities, felt the same. It was like the arm of a giant being slapping him into awareness.

He was *AWARE*.

The holographic diagrams on the Info-Cast continued to swirl, but Kael was already kilometers away in his thoughts, the seed of a revolutionary idea taking root.

From far away, he could barely hear his mother asking him if he was alright.

Then suddenly, he was back in his body. The world snapped into sharp, tactile focus, and the bowl of snacks he’d been holding shattered on the floor, scattering crumbs and synth-nuts everywhere. Before Kael could even register the mess, Gus, ever efficient, was already walking over, extending an articulated arm with a miniature vacuum attachment.

"Kael, are you alright?" Varyn was by his side in an instant, her hand gently touching his arm, her expression etched with concern. Corvin was already moving to assist Gus, his gaze sharp with worry as he looked from the mess to his son. "What happened? You just… froze up there."

Kael shook his head, the lingering echoes of the "awareness" still humming in his mind. "Yes, I’m fine," he mumbled, a faint blush rising to his cheeks as Gus efficiently vacuumed the last of the synth-nuts. "I just... I had an idea."

He looked from his mother’s concerned face to his father’s expectant one, then back to the Info-Cast, which was now showing a diagram listing the fine points of this radical discovery.

"This," he paused to look back to his internal database of awareness, "quantum entanglement, they said that the particles share a coequal state of being, instantaneously. That if you observe one, the other changes. And that if you charge one, the other takes the same charge." Kael’s eyes brightened, losing focus as the excitement of the idea took over. "But what if... what if it was more than just state or charge? What if it were information? If you could impress a pattern, a sequence of data, onto one particle, would the other instantly reflect that data, no matter how far apart they were?"

Corvin let out a slow whistle in amazement and understanding.

Varyn frowned, doubtful but intrigued. "Information transfer? Without a conduit? That’s... impossible, Kael. As your father said, the energy required to encode and transmit even a single byte through such a method would be insurmountable."

"But Zandil didn’t say the energy was excessive in his demonstration," Kael countered, turning to his father. "He used an old laser grid and a salvaged crystal. He put it together with scraps he salvaged from junk piles and discarded tech. So he must have figured out a way around that. What if the energy isn’t required for transmission, but just for linking? Once they’re linked, the information just... is." He paused, his gaze sweeping over his parents. "Imagine. If we could transmit data instantly, across any distance, without a beam or a fiber. No lag. No interference. Maybe we could build a communication device that doesn’t rely on light speed?"

Corvin’s initial skepticism began to soften, replaced by a glint of the same intellectual hunger that drove his own innovations. Instantaneous, undetectable data transfer wasn’t a communications project; it was the only way to monitor the fatal crack in the shield.

Finally, Corvin spoke, a thoughtful expression on his face. "Well. That could just be something. Varyn, do any of your contacts in the bio-science division have connections in physics? Maybe we could find out where this Zandil lives and pay him a friendly visit." He smiled at his family of brilliant minds.

"I don’t know. I’ll ask around tomorrow," Varyn said, rising from the soft couch with a stretch and a loud yawn. "But for now, it’s late. Let’s hit the tube." She walked towards the stairs. "Good night, starlight. Love you."

"Love you too, Mom," Kael called to her retreating shadow.

Soon, the apartment was quiet. Kael, Corvin, and Varyn each entered their personal sleep tubes. Kael waited a little while longer before closing his lid, hoping that a flash of awareness would hit him again, but the silence remained unbroken.

Only the faint hum of Rixie’s charging port outside his door filled the room.

He closed the lid and was fast asleep.
