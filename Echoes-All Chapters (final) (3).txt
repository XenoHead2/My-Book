Chapter One - The Unseen Child
The twin moons, known as the Two Sisters, Leeta and Orin, hung low in the southern night sky of the planet Dorin.
Kael Solan, son of Vira, not yet fourteen Dorinian orbital cycles into his life, stood at the balcony rail. The faint light of the moon outlined his slight, small body. He stood rigid, hands resting lightly on the cold nanoweave-steel railing, appearing small and thoughtful in the enveloping night, still in his sleepwear, a long, simple gray tunic worn over matching short pants. They offered little protection against the cooling night air, yet he didn't seem to notice. He was fixed on the sky, his light-red skin catching the faint, lunar glow of the Two Sisters as he quietly absorbed the vastness around him.
He gazed out at the stars and the vastness of space, the soft moonlight playing across his upturned face. He yawned and stretched, imagining all the wonders that could be out there.
The twin moons cast their spectral light, reflecting the blush of Kael's skin. All Dorinian people carried the crimson inheritance, their skin a living spectrum of red that shifted from dark, ancestral hues to the lightest pinks. Kael's was a gentle shade of soft, pale red, which he inherited from his mother. His small, unformed ear tips were the clearest sign that he was still far from reaching the age of Dorinian male maturity.
The night was still and calm, as always, with not a breath of wind. Nothing could permeate the protective dome surrounding the city, and the air smelled only of the usual recycled, filtered oxygen. But it was home.
Above Veridia, the colossal energy and nanoweave-steel dome, a defiant shield against their brutal sun, refracted the moonlight, making the structure twinkle and shimmer like a blanket of stars. From where Kael stood, he could see the giant spire of the central Crystalline Nexus. Its interlocking, transparent panels of hyperglass supported by the nanowoven metal shimmered in the moonlight.
This marvel of ancient technology serves as the primary energy hub for the entire city. Energy is harvested from the harsh environment: the giant red sun that is slowly killing the planet and the planet itself, and it powers everything under the dome.
Kael moved his gaze to the east and saw The Solarium, a vast, cubed greenhouse teeming with flora and fauna rescued from a dying world that had become incompatible with life.
Yet, for all its magnificence, Veridia was but one of many isolated sanctuaries.
Across the ravaged face of Dorin, other domed cities did exist, more stronghold bastions of life, but they had fallen silent millennia ago. The relentless, ever-worsening desert had devoured the ancient pathways and communication grids, severing all ties. Each city now stood alone, a solitary heartbeat on a dying planet, with no word or knowledge of the others for countless generations. Even the names of the other cities have been lost to time.
He returned to the endless expanse of stars and blackness for a long moment, a familiar, overwhelming thought swelling within him: his own life, his city, even his dying world, were but an insignificant speck in that vast cosmic tapestry. None of them really mattered in the enormous scope of the cosmos. Even the life and death of his entire planet and race was a blink of an eye in eternity.
Is there another child, light-years away, watching those same distant pinpricks of fire, thinking the same thoughts?
A soft, almost imperceptible hum broke his reverie.
From the shadowed hallway, his mechanical sister, Rixie, hovered into the moonlight. Her smooth, round body floated above the balcony floor, head height to Kael, suspended by nearly invisible anti-gravity pads. Her two slender, multi-jointed arms extended from her sides, capable of delicate articulation, and ended in nimble manipulators. A small, round anthropomorphic head, designed for approach-ability, tilted slightly.
"Kael," Rixie's voice chirped, a high, child-like cadence that carried a surprising warmth. "Mother said it’s time to come down and eat with the family." Her ocular inputs blinked slowly.
Kael smiled at her, the cosmic questions fading, replaced by a comfort so ingrained it was like breathing. He couldn't remember a time before Rixie. She was his shadow, his confidante, his first and only friend.
The city of Veridia was governed by a strict, unforgiving anti-population mandate, restricting families to a single approved child. Kael’s very existence was a crime, punishable by exile, separation, and, in extreme circumstances, even death. This was a life-threatening secret his parents had chosen to protect. This transgression forced Kael into a clandestine solitude, barred from the world outside his home until he reached adulthood.
In that isolated life, Rixie was his unwavering companion, the only other "child" in his isolated world, her metal heart beating in perfect sync with his own. She was built shortly after his birth, a Vigilae unit. 
Vigilae are the highest form of Dorinian mechanical life. They are fully sentient, self-aware, and independent citizens who are capable of emotions, creativity, ambition, and self-motivation. They hold equal rights as biological Dorinian, are treated as co-equal citizens, and cannot be owned. Vigilae are nearly impossible to reprogram; a full system wipe is usually required and is considered a form of murder on Dorin, illegal except in extreme circumstances. 
Kael's father, Corvin Solan, son of Bitron, a brilliant engineer, had to apply his most proprietary detail to Rixie, a task considered ethically complex on Dorin. Their designation sets them above the other mechanical tiers. She was truly one of a kind, and the whole family cherished her as much as she cherished them.
Rixie served as both a constant presence to ease his loneliness and a tireless watchman when his parents were away. Kael’s father, Corvin Solan, a brilliant electrical and mechanical engineer, had reprogrammed her with specific, proprietary detail, a task nearly impossible and considered ethically complex on Dorin due to the Vigilae's person hood. She was truly one of a kind, and the whole family cherished her as much as she cherished them.
"Alright, Rixie. Thank you. Tell her I will come right down." Kael turned for one last look at the sky.
"Ok," she said as she drifted back into the house, "Hurry up, the Kitchen is nearly done with weaving the food."
Kael pulled himself away from the railing, the vast, cold questions of the cosmos receding. He stepped back into the softly lit living space, the silence of his private contemplation giving way to the gentle hum of the house's internal systems. He moved towards the archway leading to the central staircase, his footsteps quiet on the padded metal.
His bare hand brushed the cool, smooth metal surface of the banister, and for a moment, his mind drifted again. He imagined the army of nanite constructors endlessly depositing atoms, layer by invisible layer, to build and strengthen the structure's nanoweave-steel backbone. An intricate, invisible dance, a masterpiece of precision.
The world around him seemed to drop out from under him with an audible, bone-deep growl that forced his mind to a different plane. His body went stiff, his hands and fingers splayed out like an electric shock had just attacked his flesh. His vision spun and tightened to a tiny point, then swelled back with a rush of endorphins, and stars filled his eyes.
The minor daydream of nanobots weaving atoms of metal together suddenly intensified. It stopped being a thought and became a mandatory calculation. He didn't just see the microscopic machines; he was forced to process the millions of simultaneous atomic depositions required to stabilize the specific alloy woven together to take a shape and keep it. 
It was too fast, too much data.
The pressure was instantaneous and physical. A burning, searing pain lanced through his forehead, a surge of pain running through his skull. He cried out, a choked, involuntary gasp, and instinctively brought his other hand up, trying to claw the pressure out of his temples.
Not again. 
He was starting to associate this blinding pain with every true breakthrough.
He stumbled back from the stairs, vision blurring not from dizziness, but from the sheer computational overload. In that panicked second, he instinctively traced a complex geometric solution, the patterns of energy and nano-flow, into the air before him with a trembling finger, his mind solving the impossible problem even as his body failed.
When the moment broke, the world rushed back in. The floor rushed back up to meet him, his sight cleared, and the patterns faded from view. He was leaning against the wall, his breathing ragged, his heart hammering against his ribs. The intense strain left him cold, soaked in sweat, and utterly spent. 
He felt a warm, sticky liquid run from his nose. He lifted a finger to his upper lip; the blood was dark red. He hadn't blacked out, this time,  but the physical cost was clear.
"Kael? Are you coming?" his mother's voice, Varyn's, called from below, a gentle query that broke the spell.
Kael shook his head, wiped his nose, and descended the stairs, the low hum of the house's life-support systems growing louder as he approached the Kitchen. It wasn't merely a room with appliances; it was a meticulously integrated, finely tuned culinary unit, where sleek, reflective surfaces met hidden mechanisms and conduits.
The family dinner table was thick with the sharp smells of ozone and spices and cooking protein. The kitchen’s core processing unit, a smooth, obsidian console built into the far wall, emitted a soft final chime. Varyn and Corvin were at the polished table, their faces softened by the warm ambient light of a simulated fireplace. Rixie hovered patiently beside Kael’s usual chair, her optical sensors fixed on the central culinary console, which Kael had taken to calling simply Kitchen.
Above Kitchen, a swarm of tiny, silent drones zipped through the air, their optical sensors pinpointing specific canisters of exotic seasonings, a dozen small flying robots for hands. Each miniature craft zipped to the console, deposited its measured offering, then swiftly returned to its charging port. As the last aroma of the freshly prepared meal wafted through the air, three more drones detached, each bearing a steaming plate. They glided with practiced grace to the table, slowly lowering the meals before Kael, Varyn, and Corvin.
A resonant, female voice, emanating from the kitchen’s central console, filled the room. "Please enjoy your meal," it said pleasantly, and the console powered down.
Kael slid into his seat, the soft cushion conforming to his form, and picked up his eating utensils. "Evening," he murmured, glancing between his parents.
"How was work today, dear?" Corvin asked Varyn, his own hands already moving to his meal.
Varyn took a delicate sip from her cup, her eyes alight with a familiar scientific zeal. Her face was blushing a slightly darker red.
"Remarkable, Corvin! I truly think I'm about to nail that recombination of ancestral DNA and microbial bacteria. And that culture of samples from the ancients... who knows what we can learn from them?" She turned to Kael, a proud smile on her lips. "It was quite the day."
"That's incredible, Mother!" Kael said, genuinely impressed. "Imagine what that could mean for us, for the city, and our understanding of where we all come from."
"Such a smart boy." She beamed at her son.
Varyn's smile widened before she turned her attention to her husband. "And you, Corvin? Did you finally figure out what was wrong with the generator in Section 42?"
Corvin sighed, a brief, weary sound, before a faint smile touched his lips. "It appears so. A persistent energy fluctuation, but I believe I've re-calibrated the flow regulators correctly. It should hold."
He then looked at Kael, an amused glint in his eye. "What about you, son? How was your 'day at work'?" Friendly sarcasm hung in the air.
Kael picked at the greens on his plate. "Oh, you know," he mumbled, a hint of annoyance seeping into his voice. "Just another thrilling day of solitary contemplation and extensive reading in my room. The highlight was debating ancient philosophical texts with the wall." He responded with more good-natured sarcasm.
Rixie, who had settled silently beside his chair, emitted a sharp, almost indignant chirp. Her small head tilted, and her optical sensors glowed a brighter blue.
"Kael! You were not alone! I was there! We discussed the history of telescopic exploration of our galaxy for over two hours!" She let out a small grunt and crossed her arms.
Kael winced, immediately regretting his words. He reached out a hand, gently stroking Rixie’s smooth casing.
"You're absolutely right, Rixie. I'm so sorry. That was thoughtless of me. You were wonderful company, as always," he smiled at her.
Rixie's sensors softened, dimming back to their usual hue, a soft purring noise filling her body.
"Apology accepted, Kael. And your insights on the former star charts were, as usual, quite insightful."
Varyn chuckled a soft, warm sound, "Rixie is always an excellent tutor and classmate combined. So, Kael, speaking of studies and star charts. What mysteries of the cosmos are you currently delving into?" She poured another glass of wine for herself.
Corvin nodded, his interest piqued. "Are you still exploring the theories of stellar collapse, perhaps? Or have you moved onto exoplanetary signs of life or viability?"
Kael started to answer, but his gaze fell on the small, elegant dispenser of micro-nutrient seasoning at the center of the table. He reached for it, his fingers, still small and slender, straining to reach across the polished surface with his short child’s arms.
"Allow me, Kael," Rixie chirped, before he could ask. She zipped forward, her arm extending with fluid grace to grasp the dispenser, gently placing it within his easy reach.
"Thanks, Rixie," Kael let out a frustrated grunt, accepting the bottle. He gave it a shake, watching the finely granulated powder swirl within.
"Easy with that, starlight." Varyn cautioned, her smile warm, "Too much of that will give your tummy a rumble. Remember…"
"Balance in all things," Kael said in unison.
Kael nodded. He understood the situation.
Their meals, while flavorful and perfectly nutritious, very little of the basic ingredients ever saw a field or earth to grow in a traditional, natural way. Growing sprawling hydroponic gardens or raising hundreds of livestock would consume invaluable land space and resources inside the dome, a luxury a world clinging to existence simply couldn't afford. Most of what they ate was cloned, or synthetic, organically printed from base elements into complex, digestible compounds by the kitchen's core unit, which essentially performs a continuous chain of cold fusion to bind raw matter into their daily sustenance.
"So, the cosmos, Kael?" Corvin prompted gently, pulling his son back from his brief rumination on dinner's origins. "What theories are captivating you tonight?" he questioned through a mouthful of food.
"Corvin!" Varyn scolded, "Swallow first." And he obeyed.
"Well," Kael began, his earlier frustration completely forgotten, replaced by his characteristic intellectual fervor.
"I've been reading the data on galactic redshifts. It's amazing how consistently everything out there is moving away from us, and from each other. If we trace it back, it can only mean one starting point, a moment of cosmic birth, where all matter and energy just exploded out!"
He thrust his arms out wide and made a small sound in a mock explosion.
His parents let out impressed-sounding gasps. Rixie giggled quietly.
"The universe has been getting bigger, really fast, ever since! Even though we have to see the universe through the dome, our telescopes allow us to see its beginning. It’s very exciting," he said, smiling and shoving his mouth full of food, his eyes still saying he was light-years away.
Corvin nodded slowly, a great pride settling in his eyes.
Varyn fell into the old pits of sorrow and regret. A familiar ache, sharp and persistent, tightened in her chest.
Her son Kael was an unseen child, hidden from a world that could barely sustain itself. Children could only be born with official approval, a permit granted only after a stringent assessment of need and resources. Her mind drifted to the empty years, the silent ache for a lost child. They had followed the rules, had sought and gained an approved permit for a single precious pregnancy years ago. But the harsh world they lived in had taken its toll. The complications had been swift, brutal, and tragically common among couples dreaming of a child. A life, hoped for and approved, but ultimately lost.
But they still needed a child, desperately.
So, they had risked everything for Kael.
Kael finished, his mind still buzzing with the excitement of intellectual discovery. He looked between his parents, who were both focused on him.
"You guys are being weird." Kael made a face of disgust and pushed his plate forward, "Mother, may I be excused from dinner?" he asked. Already planning his next discovery, "I want to go back up to the roof. The sky is perfect to use my telescope tonight."
"Of course, son. Be careful on the stairs. They can be slippery." Corvin smiled.
Varyn reached over and squeezed Kael's hand briefly.
"Don't stay out too late. Morning comes sooner than normal tomorrow. And we have to be in our Torpor chambers a little early to make sure we have enough time to fully regenerate tomorrow."
The Torpor Chambers were at the heart of every Dorinian home, a testament to their mastery over biology and environment. Tucked away in a quiet, temperature-controlled alcove, the individual translucent pods stood vertically, glowing with a soft, inviting cerulean light. They weren't just for sleep; they were sophisticated rejuvenation units, designed to counteract the subtle, insidious effects of prolonged existence within the closed ecosystem of the dome and to specifically facilitate their nocturnal active cycle. Once inside, the user would be enveloped in a gentle, cool mist, and a low hum would begin, signifying the chamber's activation. Their vital signs would stabilize, heart rate and neural activity slowing to a near-dormant state. During this torpor, micro-drones would meticulously scan every cell, repairing cellular damage, regenerating tissues, and flushing out metabolic waste products that accumulated over the active cycle. Hormonal balances were subtly recalibrated, energy reserves replenished, and the very structure of their bones and muscles reinforced against atrophy.
The chambers also acted as a mental reset. The low-frequency neural pulses emitted by the chamber would filter out the day's accumulated stress, reordering memories and enhancing cognitive functions, ensuring each Dorinian awoke refreshed, alert, and ready for their next active cycle. It was a perfect, controlled environment, allowing them to thrive and sustain their long lifespans, optimized for their specific needs within the dome.
"I won't. Come on, Rixie, let’s explore space!" Kael slid out of his chair. He pushed Rixie ahead of him, who chirped happily, and hurried away, his light footsteps receding up the stairs. "Bye!" he called over his shoulder.
Corvin called after him, "And don’t let me catch you trying to sneak out again tomorrow!" But Kael was long gone up the stairs.
"He heard you," Varyn said with no confirmation and kissed her husband's cheek.
A comfortable silence settled over the table, punctuated only by the quiet hum of the house. Varyn and Corvin looked at each other, a shared language passing between their eyes. Corvin reached across the polished surface of the table, covering Varyn's hand with his own. He squeezed gently, his thumb tracing the back of her knuckles.
"Varyn," he murmured, his voice soft, attuned to the subtle tension he sensed in her. His eyes, deep and understanding, met hers. "Anything done out of that much love, with that much hope, can only be a good thing." He tightened his grip on her hand.
"Our son is absolutely brilliant, and once he's grown up a bit and can pass for an adult, he will slip into society unnoticed in a city this size. He'll have a happy life. Maybe even make a family of his own." A small, hopeful smile touched his lips.
Varyn's eyes welled, blurring Corvin's face, and a tear escaped, tracing a path down her cheek.
"Yes," she whispered, her voice thick with emotion. "I know, Corvin. I just... I just wish he could be a normal kid sometimes."
The silence returned, heavier now, filled with the unspoken weight of their love and sacrifice.


2- Lessons In Silence
Chapter Two - Lessons In Silence
Kael woke with a tremor of anticipation, a feeling usually reserved for the rare, scheduled deep-space observation nights.
Today, however, his focus wasn't on distant stars, but on the forbidden world just beyond his front door. The plan was the culmination of three failed attempts and 147 days of methodical data logging.
His earliest efforts had been brute-force and naive. The electronic locks on the upper-level balconies were secured by a basic, randomized bioelectric handshake that defied his efforts to log or predict. The structural seals were made of Nanoweave-Iron, a material impervious to the small, scavenged tools he could fabricate. He even briefly considered the primary ventilation shafts, only to be deterred by Rixie's constant atmospheric diagnostics; any subtle air disruption would be flagged by her processors instantly.
His successful strategy, however, was a triumph of applied psychological engineering. Kael realized the only exploit in the house’s security grid was his father, Corvin Solan, a brilliant engineer, but predictably fallible in mundane, simple ways. Corvin occasionally used the external service hatch for automated drone maintenance. Kael had observed and timed the window: during the 78.4 seconds it took the external airlock to cycle and equalize pressure. When his hands were full of reports, data pads, or other work-related cargo, Corvin sometimes propped the inner hatch open using a precisely shaped synth-plastic wedge. This prevented a redundant internal pressure lock but also, critically, took the door's biometric scanner temporarily but functionally offline. 
For two full weeks, Kael waited for the perfect moment, when his father had his arms full, and this specific, fragile window to open to him. And now he had his own, perfectly crafted wedge ready.
He waited, listening to the familiar morning rhythms of the house: the soft whir of the meal synthesizers on the lower level, his father Corvin’s low murmur, then his mother Varyn’s brighter, more energetic replies. Soon, the gentle hiss of the airlock signaled their departure for their research sectors.
His own room, usually a sanctuary of knowledge and solitude, had recently felt like a cage. He traced the smooth, cool surface of his wall, imagining the bustling corridors and towering structures outside. He was almost an adult by their world’s accelerated standards, yet still confined, still unseen.
Today, that would change.
Finally, the faint thump of the external airlock cycling reached him, followed by the complete hush of the house.
They were both gone.
Rixie hovered, waiting outside Kael’s room for him to wake up and start the day, when she heard faint shuffling and movement from behind Kael’s door.
‘What is that boy doing? He sounds busy. This is highly irregular,’ She registered.
She knocked gently on his door. "Kael? Are you awake in there?"
He lets out a bark of surprise, "Oh! Um, Yeah, I, um…just got up," he stammered. The shuffling and movement intensified. Rixie’s internal threat monitoring routines leapt into overclock mode.
"Kael, I’m coming in," she said, reaching for the door release.
"No!" he shouted. "Don’t! I’m…I’m naked! Yes. You don’t want to see that."
"Don’t be silly, little brother. Nothing I haven’t seen before. Besides, I am not some flighty organic. I think I can handle it." She laughed and pressed the door release.
It slid into the wall with a hiss.
Kael was not, in fact, naked, but fully clothed, wearing black, tight-fitting clothes and a similarly colored cover on his head; a cloth bag sat on his bed. He was mid-shove, stuffing a blanket in the bag. She whizzed over and looked inside.
Kael sighed, stopped his efforts, hung his arms down, and looked at the floor, his face turning the dark red of shame.
Rixie’s internal processors whirred, cycling through probabilities at a frantic rate. She floated there briefly, shocked into silence by what she saw in the bag.
‘Warm clothes, a large blanket, food, a portable illumination unit, a small telescope, and a map! This is not consistent with standard morning study protocols. This is highly irregular indeed! No, this is trouble,’ she deduced.
Her optical sensors flared to a frantic red, and her voice, usually a pleasant chime, ratcheted up several octaves, sounding less like a helpful companion and more like a high-pitched alarm.
"Kael! What is the meaning of this?! Are you trying to…to leave the house!?" She sounded like a frazzled sibling who’d just caught her brother trying to sneak out for a late-night snack with a grappling hook and a bag full of explosives. "Are you going to have a picnic in the ventilation shafts? Because believe me, the air stinks, and it is covered in dust! You would be wheezing for days if you went there."
Her sarcasm hung smugly in the air as she crossed her delicate manipulator arms in a gesture of disbelief.
Kael took a step towards the bed. She zipped backward so fast she nearly bumped into the wall, her delicate manipulator arms flailing.
"You do not want to know, Rix," he admitted, recognizing he’d been caught.
She suddenly sagged a little in the air. "Oh no. Are you running away?" she asked, sounding very sad. Her little motors whined down in pitch.
The hyper-efficient, safety-oriented big sister was very worried inside and out. Her internal coalition algorithms screamed catastrophe!
Kael looked as shocked as he could make himself look. "Don’t be dramatic, Rixie," Kael muttered, trying to tuck the map under a tunic.
"I do not get dramatic. This is a direct violation of Mom and Dad’s rules! Think of the risks! The potential for social interaction without proper safeguards! Dramatic?!" she shrieked, hovering frantically from side to side. "I am your sister! I love you, we all love you! You can’t run away!"
‘His vital signs are registering an elevated excitement level, which, combined with this contraband satchel, indicates a clear deviation from optimal safety parameters! My internal heat is rising in distress,’ she registered.
"What?" He looked shocked. "Of course, I am not running away. I’m just going on a little…field trip around the area. Just some time to myself, take in the city, see the stars from somewhere new." She beeped in alarm, "Not far! Just a little walk around. I have to get out of this house for a little while. You do realize I have never been out of it, right?" He knew she realized it.
"It’s not safe, K! If someone sees you, it will break our whole family apart. We would all never see each other again! But even if you get back without being seen, Mom and Dad are going to be very angry with you. And with me for letting you go." She folded her arms in front of her, "The risk is just too high. I forbid you to go out there on some solo adventure."
Kael broke into a devious grin, "So come with me." This was his plan all along, and he would feel much safer with her around. But if he made a play of going alone, there was no way she would let him go alone.
"Oh no, I will not encourage you in this." She spun around and put her back to Kael in defiance.
He sighed deeply in mock resolution. "Well, I guess I will have to go alone. Oh well, hope no one sees me, or murders me and eats me. Goodbye, dear sister whom I love; please tell Mom and Dad I love them if I don’t make it back." He chuckled, zipped up the bag, and mounted it on his shoulder.
"OH!" She spun around to face him. "That’s not fair!" He could see her circuits firing in every direction as she tried to think of a way to talk him out of it. "Fine, I will go with you. But only to watch your back and make sure no one sees you or…" she paused for effect, "eats you, or something worse." She made a visible shiver over her entire body.
"Worse than being eaten?" he grinned at her.
"Yes. Fine, so where are we going then?" She made a loud, very noticeable sigh.
Mischief spread all over his face with a crooked smile.
"Out," he said flatly and walked out of the room.
Kael’s flat declaration hung in the air, leaving Rixie with no choice but to follow. With another exasperated and subtly worried internal whir, she engaged her null-gravity pads and zipped after him.
The journey from Kael’s bedroom to the external service hatch, a route only ever used by his parents for maintenance checks, was a tightrope walk of silent, calculated movements for Rixie. Every shadow seemed to harbor a lurking threat, every distant hum a potential monitor. Kael, meanwhile, moved with an almost reckless joy, his eyes wide with discovery as they navigated the narrow, utilitarian corridors and stairwells of their isolated section of the house.
A blast of crisp, filtered air, subtly different from the recycled air of their home, washed over them. They took in new smells all around them: flowers in bloom, exhaust from factories, the sharp ozone emitted from countless hovering transports around the city, all held in by the ever-vigilant dome above them. Kael took a deep, exhilarating breath. Savoring every new olfactory sensation.
The sounds of the city, usually muted to a distant hum inside their home, now swelled into a complex, cacophonous symphony: the whisper of automated transport, the distant clatter of machinery, and the muted murmur of unseen voices. A living, breathing city opened up before them.
Kael was rapturous with joy and wonder, his eyes closed, his mouth open with a huge smile, he spread his arms out wide at his side, and took it all in.
Rixie, on the other hand, floating behind him, registered a state of high alarm and caution. She emitted a series of rapid, low-frequency warning beeps that only Kael could hear. Her sensors scanned as far as they could reach, on the lookout for any signs of life or death.
"Kael! Oh my, there are so many things out here! My ears are buzzing with all this weird stuff! And it’s kinda gross, all this dust! We shouldn’t be doing this! Please, Kael, I’m scared. Oh no! I'm detecting unprocessed organic matter! A bio-hazard!" she practically shrieked, shrinking slightly more behind him, her internal gyros subtly trembling.
Kael ignored her, pushing the hatch wider. He stepped out into a narrow, public service alley, squinting at the sheer, overwhelming scale of the city.
Towering structures of void-steel and translucent synth-glass pierced an artificial sky, glowing with a soft, pervasive light from embedded solar panels, casting lights of all colors onto the underside of the dome’s surface. The colossal structure hummed with a deep, resonant thrum that vibrated through the ground beneath his feet and up into his very bones. A hum Rixie's internal sensors identified as the pulsed discharge of the high-energy induction coils regulating the field, which acted as the protector, reflecting light down into the city in a shimmering wave of colors, bathing everything in its radiance.
Moving walkways hummed with quiet efficiency, ferrying citizens through the grand thoroughfares that stretched as far as the eye could see. It was nothing like the limited, controlled views from his high balcony; it was a sprawling, vibrant, alive world.
"Whoa," Kael breathed, his voice barely a whisper, completely captivated. "It’s… it’s amazing. It’s beautiful." His eyes were wide with wonder.
"I only see an ugly, dirty, smelly city. And amazing? Ha! More like, dangerous." Rixie hissed, shrinking further behind him. "Every moment we are outside, we are closer to being discovered. Someone will see us, mark my words, and they will catch us, and they will eat you, well, eat you anyway." She let the final words drip slowly out of her vocal processor. "Where are we going now? Isn’t this enough outside for now? Why see it all in one night, right?" She tried to reason with him.
"There is something called a park." He made the word sound like the most opulent and wondrous place ever created. He fanned his hands out in front of him in a dramatic gesture. "We are going there first," and he started walking away from the house where he was safe.
"Why are we doing that?" she whined. But Kael was already moving down the alley, drawn by a distant patch of vibrant green and the high-pitched, joyous sounds that carried on the air. "What do you mean by ‘first’?" She interrogated pointlessly.
"Come on, Rixie! I hear something!"
"Me too, that’s exactly why we should not go that way." But she knew it was pointless.
He set off, a blur of red skin and boundless energy, heading straight toward the sounds of laughter and play. Rixie, with a final, mournful whir, zipped frantically after him, her primary directive and love for her brother, overriding her deep-seated fear.
The sounds of laughter grew louder, pulling Kael like a magnet. The duo of escapees emerged from the alleyway onto a wide, curving path that bordered a vast expanse of manicured greenery.
This was the park, an impossible burst of vibrant life in the heart of the metal and glass city. Soft, spongy grass, a hue of green Kael had only seen in holographic simulations, stretched out underfoot. Towering, spiral-cut trees, engineered for efficient air purification, cast intricate patterns of shade.
Kael bent and caressed the living, green grass with his fingers.
And there they were: three small figures. Red-tinted skin, lithe short limbs, hair the color of polished obsidian or shimmering moonlight. They darted and weaved across the glen, shrieking with delight, a blur of motion and pure, unadulterated joy.
They weren’t fighting or studying or doing anything Kael recognized from his limited understanding of normal. They were simply running, chasing each other, their movements loose and unburdened. He had read about it, but never seen or experienced it.
Kael stopped dead, mesmerized. He had seen adults, of course, from his balcony and in his parents’ projections, but never another child.
Not one.
"Kids. They are kids, like me." Kael whispered, hypnotized by the play, and wandered slowly out of hiding behind a large tree."Why are they just running around like that? Aren’t they worried they will get caught, too?" He looked at Rixie in confusion.
"They are not like you, Kael," she had been dreading this conversation for years. "Those kids are sanctioned. They are legal, K. Mom and Dad broke the law to have you. That’s how much they needed you, little brother." She placed a hand on Kael’s cheek and let out a low, sad tone.
"Yeah," he said flatly and turned his back to her to survey the children who were allowed to live free, "I just didn’t know that there were other kids in the city."
A profound, aching loneliness settled over him, mingling strangely with a burst of pure, envious wonder. Rixie hovered anxiously at his shoulder, her optical sensors scanning the unfamiliar activity, her internal processors screaming warnings. He took a few tentative steps towards the group, all caution seemingly flushed from his mind. Sadness and curiosity played across his face.
"Kael! We are too exposed right now! We need to get out of here, now!" she hissed, her voice tight with panic. She gently gripped Kael’s shoulders and tried to lightly pull him back without initiating a forced restraint protocol.
Kael barely registered her words. He shrugged off her hands and took a big step forward, then another, drawn by the irresistible pull of shared youth. He wanted to understand, to join in, to experience whatever magic they were weaving.
He moved slowly, barely trying to be invisible, inching closer to get a better look, while Rixie tugged desperately at the hem of his tunic.
"No! Get back!" she whispered, her voice a frantic buzz.
One of the children, a girl who seemed to be his age and with hair like spun moonlight, took a sharp, unexpected turn in his direction, laughing all the while.
"You can’t catch me! La la la la!" she sang, her voice bright and carefree.
She suddenly skidded to a halt just a few meters from where Kael was failing to hide. The girl’s eyes, wide with playful triumph, suddenly locked onto Kael’s awestruck face.
"Oh," the child said, her laughter fading into open-mouthed surprise. "Hello. Who are you?" she asked with a huge grin on her face.
Kael stammered, opened his mouth, then closed it again, unable to speak. He looked at her in shock and wonder. Time seemed to freeze for him as he looked at her; he thought she must be the most beautiful person who had ever lived. Her eyes sparkled like stars, her hair flowed like liquid light spilling down past her soft shoulders, and her skin was deep red and shone as if it held its own sun inside her small body.
She spun around, shouting, into her cupped hands. "Hey, everyone! Come quickly! It’s another kid!"
A moment of stunned silence descended upon Kael and Rixie. Then, a shared, primal scream tore from both their mouths. They turned as one, Kael’s short legs pumping furiously, Rixie’s anti-gravity pads humming as she zipped into a panicked retreat.
"Wait! Don’t go! We thought we were the only three kids in the whole city! Come play with us!" the children shouted after them, their voices echoing with genuine excitement and confusion, not malice.
Rixie, hearing their rapidly approaching footsteps and the growing volume of their pleas, turned her head 180 degrees, her optical sensors confirming the little monsters were indeed quickly gaining. Her internal processors shrieked a new warning: Proximity breach imminent! Evasion protocol engaged!
With a swift, decisive move, she reached over, scooped Kael up into her arms tightly, lifting him off the ground.
She then hit the top speed she could reach with such a burden; she was a nearly silent blur streaking across the field. They left the park behind as quickly as she could travel, not daring to look back, though Rixie could still hear the children’s calls, now fading, but still filled with longing.
Rixie, with Kael clutched tightly in her arms, didn’t stop until the joyous, yet terrifying, shouts of the other children were completely inaudible. Only then did she gently lower Kael to the ground, her own anti-gravity pads settling her beside him.
They both collapsed onto the cold stone of a deserted alleyway. Kael was gasping and laughing with a mixture of terror and exhilarating novelty, Rixie’s internal fans whirring loudly as her systems cooled.
"See?!" Rixie huffed, her voice tight with a triumphant yet shaky edge. "I told you someone would see us! And those were just kids! Can you imagine if their parents had been around? Do you even care what would happen to Mom and Dad? Or to me? They’d be taken away, Kael! I would be decommissioned! And you’d be…you’d be…" She trailed off, her optical sensors flickering with the catastrophic possibilities her processors were projecting.
Still breathing heavily, he laughed loudly and seemed not to hear her. "That was amazing," he said. His hand was already in his bag, pulling out the folded map. His eyes scanned it, tracing lines and symbols with an intellectual intensity Rixie knew well.
"Aha!" he exclaimed, pointing to a large, prominent circle on the map. "This is what we see next!"
Rixie zipped forward, peering over his shoulder. Her internal gyros spun.
‘Location identification: Shield Dome eastern wall edge. Containment breach potential: Extreme. Parental directive violation: Catastrophic.’
"Next?!" she shrieked, her voice echoing off the alley walls. "Next?! Kael, we need to go home right now! This is dangerous! Beyond dangerous!"
Kael cut her off with a wave of his hand, already getting to his feet and hitching the knapsack higher on his shoulder.
"This way," he said, already moving towards the alley’s exit, his green eyes gleaming with renewed purpose. "You worry too much."
Kael’s impatience pulled Rixie from her panic. With a sigh that whirred through her internal mechanisms, she followed. The narrow service alley soon spilled out onto a vibrant thoroughfare, unlike anything Kael had ever imagined. Automated transports, sleek and silent, glided along invisible tracks, ferrying Dorinians whose faces were a blur of expressions Kael couldn’t decipher. The air hummed with a thousand tiny energies, the whisper of fabric against skin, the distant chime of an automated vendor, the rhythmic pulse of the city’s power grid. It was a vast network of life, exhilarating and overwhelming.
Kael darted glances everywhere, his head swiveling, trying to absorb every detail. He saw shimmering storefronts displaying gravity-defying sculptures, glowing advertisements for nutrient pastes, and busy citizens moving with purpose. He noticed the faint, metallic tang of recycled air mixed with the sweet, synthetic scent of bio-processed flowers from overhead planters. Rixie, meanwhile, kept herself low and close to the ground, her internal processors working overtime to categorize every potential threat.
They navigated several busy junctions, Kael occasionally pointing to a particularly strange transport or an oddly shaped building.
Rixie would offer a reluctant grumble of "This is a bad idea," but Kael’s sheer fascination kept her from forcing a retreat. The dome, a subtle curvature in the sky from his balcony, now loomed, growing in impossible scale with every block they passed. Its surface, initially appearing smooth, resolved into a tessellated network of hexagonal energy panels, each one pulsating with a faint, contained light.
Finally, they reached a massive plaza that opened directly onto the base of the Shield Dome. It rose above them, impossibly vast, a shimmering, translucent wall of pure energy that seemed to touch the very heavens. Kael tilted his head back so far it ached, his jaw slack in awe. The colossal structure hummed with a deep, resonant thrum that vibrated through the ground beneath his feet and up into his very bones. It was the protector, the very reason their world endured, an almost divine presence.
"Wow," Kael whispered, the word lost in the dome’s silent power. He reached out a hesitant hand, as if to touch its immense, unyielding surface.
Rixie, though equally awestruck by its sheer magnificence, quickly snapped back to her primary directives. ‘Warning: High energy field proximity. Unauthorized access probability: Critical.‘
"Kael! Don’t touch it!" she barked.
His hand froze inches from the shimmering barrier. Through its translucent field, Kael could see the world beyond, or rather, the nothingness that was the world beyond. An endless, unbroken expanse of blinding white sand stretched to the horizon, a desert of desolation. There were no clouds in the perpetually pale sky, no hint of a plant, no sign of life of any kind. It was an ocean of white, seemingly as vast and empty as space itself.
No, more empty than space, Kael realized, because at least space had planets, stars, distant nebulae. This was just... oblivion. A profound sadness settled over him as he contemplated what his homeworld used to be, what it had lost.
Rixie suddenly interjected, "Look over there, K. A construction site." Her optical sensors zoomed in, drawing his attention away from the dome’s flawless, dangerous surface and toward a section of its base.
From their vantage point, they could see a massive, sprawling construction site. Automated cranes, like colossal insects, moved with silent precision, ferrying massive girders through the air. And among them, something Kael recognized from his lessons: floating rocks, sheets of hyperglass, beams of metal struts all shimmering with an eerie, contained light, defying gravity on the same null-gravity engines that Rixie had, as they slowly moved into position.
Kael’s eyes widened, a grin splitting his face. He started to walk toward the site, drawn by the spectacle. The construction workers were both metal and flesh alike. Several Dorinian and Vigilae males, all thick and stout, stood around in a group looking over a holographic, floating diagram of a short building that was partially constructed behind them. Their voices carried on the filtered air. They were laughing, joking, their interactions seamless and easy. To each other, they were just the same. Just people, working together to do an important job.
Kael pulled Rixie closer, drawn by the vibrant energy of cooperation at the site, "Look, Rixie. They are friends like you and me."
Then a large, bipedal Vigilae worker, easily twice Kael’s height, spoke up to his friends, "Hey, watch this. Hey, TR514! Come over here!"
He called out to a short, simple storage chest of a robot, with stubby legs and a hinged lid, which waddled over to the Vigilae who had called it. This was a Drone Unit, the lowest tier of mechanical life. Drones operate on simple scripting with no AI and can be programmed and reprogrammed very easily. They are considered property and move between owners and jobs regularly, often rented out as temporary helpers to both biological and Vigilae Dorinians.
It halted, waiting, and its lid silently popped open, waiting for him to take whatever tool he needed. But instead, the Vigilae worker reached out with one of his heavy, articulated legs and gave the small robot a firm shove. The little chest-bot toppled over, landing helplessly on its back, its stubby legs flailing uselessly in the air as it tried, and failed, to right itself. A roar of wild laughter and jeers erupted from all the surrounding workers.
"Those drones are so dumb," one man laughed and pointed at the supine bot.
"So are they," a biological worker pointed at a two-story-tall shiny yellow grav-crane that was busily, autonomously levitating piles of stone or sections of walls that would need the intelligent crew members to complete and install. It ignored the little helpless toolbox and paid no attention when one of the workers threw a glass bottle at it, shattering over its chassis. The Grav-Crane was a Mech Unit, which is one tier above Drones. Mechs possess a primitive AI capable of speech and following complicated verbal directives, but have no self-determination, emotions, or ambition. While also considered property, they are reprogrammed relatively easily and typically stay with one owner or family for long periods.
Rixie’s optical sensors flared crimson. Her internal systems screamed with outrage, though Kael only heard her sharp, distressed cry.
Her internal systems immediately flagged the incident as a ‘Critical social protocol deviation, triggering a non-essential emotional response. Opportunity for empathy lessons recognized. She registered.
"Oh! Kael, that’s not right!" she zipped forward, pulling hard on his tunic. "I know it's not aware of what just happened and has no feeling to hurt, no mind to even know what is happening. It’s just a toolbox that walks really. But I don’t like seeing lesser beings of any kind being mistreated! And I most certainly do have feelings, and I am feeling very upset right now. Come on, let’s get out of here!" Her voice was tight with a mixture of anger and genuine pity for the poor, abused toolbox.
Kael, startled by her intensity but absolutely agreeing with her sentiment, didn’t delay and followed her from the scene. He allowed Rixie to pull him away, away from the crude laughter and the flailing, helpless robot, and back into the flow of the city.
"One more place I want to see, and then we head home," Kael announced, pulling out his map again, his finger tracing a new symbol. "It’s supposed to be a giant hole, just filled with water."
Rixie’s null-gravity unit dipped slightly in a dismissive shrug. "Sounds perfectly boring. Come on, let’s just get it over with so we can get home already." Her internal clocks were already ticking off the growing deficit of their unauthorized absence.
They navigated the city’s complex network of thoroughfares, following Kael’s adventurous lead. The artificial lake was a massive, sunken basin of shimmering blue, and it glowed softly in the artificial light of the dome. Its banks were lined with smooth, white, stone buildings, and the air here was noticeably cooler, carrying a faint, clean scent of filtered water.
Kael and Rixie settled onto the roof of a stone building just above the lake’s edge, gazing down at the tranquil, deep and dark expanse. Kael gasped, his eyes wide. Even Rixie found herself momentarily silenced, her optical sensors adjusting to the sheer, breathtaking beauty of the vast liquid surface.
‘Analysis: Hydro-storage impressive. Aesthetic parameters: High. Threat assessment: Low.’
Their moment of quiet awe was interrupted by the creak of hard, dried clothing and the soft shuffle of feet. An old Dorinian man, his dark red skin faded with age, walked slowly to the edge of the lake directly below them. He carried a long, thin pole and, with practiced ease, cast a nearly invisible line into the water.
"What’s he doing?" Kael whispered, utterly bewildered.
"He’s…fishing," Rixie explained, her voice a low murmur. "It’s an ancient pastime. They used to catch living creatures from bodies of water for sustenance or for leisure. They now use bio-sampling drones for the activity." She realized, with a lurch of her internal gyros, that the old man was positioned perfectly, blocking their only clear path down from the building. There was no way to get to the ground without him seeing them.
A shared dread settled between them. They had to wait. Silently, motionlessly, they watched the old man. Minutes stretched into what felt like an eternity. Kael shifted, restless, but Rixie held him firm with a silent, intense stare. The old man hummed tunelessly, his gaze fixed on the water.
Then, his pole suddenly bent, and a jolt went through his arm. His face lit up with a grin.
"Ah, got one!" he exclaimed, reeling in the line with excited grunts. A silvery, analyte-retrieval drone flopped at the end of his line. He carefully unhooked it, placed it into a small, insulated chemical assay container for immediate transport and analysis. He shuffled off, disappearing around the corner of the building.
The moment he was gone, Rixie scooped Kael up without a word, her internal systems screaming: ‘Critical Time Deviation! ‘
The thrill of exploration was utterly replaced by a cold, prickling fear. They rushed, as quickly and inconspicuously as possible, through the city’s now-familiar pathways, all thoughts of adventure replaced by the urgent, overriding need to reach the safety of home.
Later that night at home, Corvin and Varyn sat on the communal couch, their shoulders almost touching, their forms slumped in a tableau of profound worry. The glow of the inactive info-screens reflected dimly in Varyn’s tear-bright eyes. It had been hours. The longer Kael was gone, the more likely the chance that they would be discovered. A quiet despair had begun to settle over the apartment, thick and suffocating.
Then, the soft hiss of the outer airlock cycling.
They both shot to their feet, hope and terror shifting on their faces, and ran to the door.
Kael, disheveled but unharmed, stumbled through the door, Rixie hovering anxiously behind him. For a breathless moment, Corvin and Varyn were filled with an overwhelming, almost dizzying joy. Varyn knelt, pulling Kael into a fierce embrace, burying her face in his hair. Corvin clapped Rixie gently on her chassis, his relief evident.
But as the initial flood of relief subsided and their parental concern re-engaged, joy quickly curdled into a potent mix of anger, disappointment, and renewed worry. They detached from their children, their faces hardening.
"Kael! Rixie! Do you have any idea what could have happened?!" Corvin’s voice, usually calm, was a low growl.
"That was incredibly dangerous! Unacceptable! We were sick with fear!" Varyn added, her voice trembling with barely controlled fury.
Kael interrupted, "Rixie is blameless. I…I tricked her into it, Mom! Dad!" he blurted out, trying to pull the blame off his sister.
Rixie, however, floated forward, her optical sensors steady, her form rigid with principle. "No," she declared, her voice firm, no trace of whine or panic. "That is not true, Kael. Mom, Dad, I went of my own free will to keep an eye on him. He is my little brother. It was my responsibility to keep him safe, even if it meant violating protocol." She floated down lower to the floor in shame. "And I am sorry to have worried you both."
Corvin and Varyn exchanged a look of pure exasperation, then turned their furious gazes back to Kael, then to Rixie, and back to Kael. The defiance from Rixie, the attempt to take the blame from Kael. It was too much.
"Enough!" Corvin boomed, his deep voice echoing in the suddenly silent apartment. "Both of you! Upstairs! Straight to bed and to your charger! We will speak about this in the morning. And believe me," he added, his voice dropping to a dangerous whisper, "we will speak about this."
The two took the stairs up to Kael’s room and Rixie’s charging port just outside his door in dejected remorse and quiet resolve.

3-A Seed Of Awareness
Chapter Three - A Seed Of Awareness
Two years had passed since Kael’s infamous "field trip," a forbidden excursion that still, occasionally, drew a sharp sigh from Mom or a stern, lingering look from Dad. The talk had been long and serious.
Eventually, life in their secluded house settled back into its familiar rhythms, though the nearly sixteen-year-old Kael was now under even closer, albeit subtly disguised, observation. He still yearned for the outside, but his boundless curiosity had found a new, equally captivating outlet: Dad’s workshop.
The workshop was a labyrinth of organized chaos. Its shelves overflowed with disassembled components, conduits coiling like metallic arms, and the faint, comforting scent of ozone and heated synth-alloys.
A small music player, always on the table, filled the room. The music was a dynamically generated auditory experience built from intricate sonic algorithms. Complex and detailed, its deep strings, bright horns, and billowing woodwinds conveyed structural data and complex mathematical patterns. The shifts in harmony and tempo created a complex sonic geometry that gave Kael a profound sense of engineered order, majestic and sad all at once. Corvin always moved his head to the rhythm of the music as he worked, saying it helped him think.
Moonlight, filtered and softened by the dome's high arch, streamed through the single, thick hyperglass window, illuminating motes of dust dancing in the air, casting everything in its shimmer.
Today, Kael wasn't observing his father's work. He was hunched over a workbench, his brow furrowed in concentration. Before him lay what looked like the carcass of a small, four-armed utility unit, its polished shell opened, its several optical sensors dark, and one of its manipulator arms bent to an unnatural angle. This was his father’s personal assistant, a complex piece of old-generation tech that Vigalea had been with his father since he was just a small ear tipless boy. But it had broken down when Varyn was pregnant with Kael and had stubbornly resisted all his father's attempts at repair. Once Kael had been born, his father had other things to occupy his time.
Corvin, a meticulous engineer, had finally stopped working at it, consigning the unit to a corner of the workshop until he could source replacement parts, a task that, for tech this old, was akin to finding data crystals in a sandstorm. Kael had seen the frustrated frown lines on his father’s face, the defeated slump of his shoulders, when he looked at his broken childhood friend, and a quiet rebellion stirred within Kael. He resolved to fix his father’s Vigilae companion.
Kael ignored the complex diagnostic schematics on the flickering data-pad beside him. Instead, he relied on intuition, a strange, almost tactile understanding of the machine's internal language. His thin red fingers, now slightly longer and more nimble, gently probed the unit's exposed circuitry. He hummed a low, tuneless melody, his head cocked, his slightly longer ear tips twitching as he listened not to sound, but to the faint, discordant feel of the broken mechanisms. He wasn't looking for a broken wire or a burnt-out relay; he was searching for the current dissonance in its energy flow. His mind processed the ebb and flow of potential energy pathways, registering a localized magnetic field variance. It seemed to him like a discordant frequency in a smooth broadcast, a signal only he could detect.
Rixie, meanwhile, floated nearby, tidying a stack of data-slates with unnecessary precision. Her internal processors were running their own diagnostics on Kael.
'Subject: Kael. Current activity: Attempted repair of damaged Vigilae unit. Based on Corvin's prior failed attempts, the probability of success: 0.007%. Conclusion: Highly inefficient, yet fascinating,' she registered.
"Still at it, little brother?" Rixie chirped, her voice light, though her optical sensors were fixed on his work. "Dad said that old thing is beyond repair. You're just wasting your time. And you promised to help me calibrate the nutrient paste dispenser today." She chidded.
Kael didn't even look up.  He hummed thoughtfully, focusing on the discordant frequency inside the dormant Vigilae. The sound faded entirely as the inner mechanics of the machine dematerialized in his mind's eye, replaced by pure data. He wasn't seeing the electronics; he was seeing the required flow of energy needed to override the localized magnetic variance. The sheer force of the required computation hit him harder than the day before.
The world dissolved. The floor seemed to fly away from him as he felt like he was floating in space.
Oh no. Here it comes again. He thought as his eyes dimmed, his hands dropped the tools they held. His face lifted, as he seemed to be staring up at the ceiling, but he was mentally no longer in that room.
Previous episodes all seemed like a nap compared to this one. 
This time, the vision was total. Thick dark green lines and patterns solidified in the air above the mechanical body. Electrons, circular patterns, holographic circuits, all came into view before his eyes. Pulsating with imagined energy and potential. He saw every complex molecular bond within the broken bot, every failed signal path; he traced each interconnected node until he found something that was not supposed to be there, something preventing the cosmic light from completing its journey. And then he saw the precise, infinitesimal point and the exact angle required to dislodge the offending particle. The information overloaded his system, slamming his mind into a full, traumatic shutdown. His brain was trying to solve a thousand calculations per second.


The last thing Kael registered was the searing pain behind his eyes and the sound of Rixie’s quiet internal processors glitching in surprise. His legs gave way, and he collapsed onto the cold workshop floor. A dull thud was the only sound in the sudden, terrifying silence.
Rixie’s emergency diagnostics screamed an immediate red alert. Her optical sensors locked onto his body, performing a full scan: 
‘Subject: Kael. Vital Signs: Erratic. Source: Unknown, but repeating neurological trauma. Action: Intervention required.’
She abandoned the data-slates, letting them clatter. Her small, powerful frame rushed to his side.
"Kael! No, no, no, not again!" she whispered fiercely, bending down. She could see the dark blood already welling up from his nose onto the pale plastone floor. This wasn’t the first time she had seen him collapse, but it never got easier. She quickly scanned the workbench, grabbed a clean rag and the glass of water Kael had been drinking, dousing the cloth. She wiped his sweaty, blood-streaked face. "I am calling them, little brother. I can’t keep this a secret. It goes against all my instincts."
He groaned, stirring slightly. His eyes fluttered open, blinking slowly. The fierce intensity in his stare was gone, replaced by a dazed exhaustion. He pushed himself up, leaning on Rixie. The entire event had lasted only a minute or two, but he felt like he had been asleep for hours.
She held his head gently. "Kael, please. I know you swore me to secrecy, but they need to know! You could—"
"Rixie... don't," he rasped, his voice rough. He wiped his nose on the back of his hand. "You promised. Look at the bright side: I found it." The disarming smile on his face made even Rixie flutter.


Rixie crossed her arms, her chest lights flashing a frustrated yellow. "A collapsed circulatory system is not a 'bright side,' Kael. Your brain is attacking itself! I am built for logic, and concealing this is illogical. They have to know."
Kael stood up, steadying himself on the workbench, already regaining his energy. He flashed a tired, genuine grin. "But I always come out of it with the answer, don't I?" He gestured to the Vigilae. "My brain is just so powerful, it needs to hit the reset button, that's all. Now, help me find my precision tool."
"That is not funny, Kael. This is not normal."
At last, he located the minuscule partial piece of sand, almost invisible to the naked eye, lodged deep within a delicate micro-coil. With the precision tool, he carefully dislodged it, then reassembled the housing and reattached the wires to feed the energy he saw floating in the air in his mind. Then he ran a charging cable from the wall and connected it to the long-dead synthetic.
"Now, we wait and see." Kael stretched his arms and back, smiling at his work.
"The problem was in the secondary modulator coil, not the primary conduit. Nothing was broken. It was just blocked, and….sticky." 
"You don't sound like you are sure about this." Rixie ran a final scan over his biosigns.
He just shrugged and sat on a stool, pulled a bit of leftover lunch from his pocket, and ate quietly.
After a while, a faint click echoed through the quiet workshop. Then, with a soft whir, activity lights on the long-dormant Vigilae began to flicker to life, then glowing with a steady, emerald light. The sound of its internal engines spinning up, electric cobwebs of sleep falling from digital eyes. Finally, the boot-up was complete, and the noises died down to a normal robotic hum. The unit emitted a slight, low beep as the boot-up diagnostics showed flickering across his optical screens.
Kael sat back, a triumphant, almost giddy grin spreading across his face. "See, Rixie? Very much worth it."
Rixie zipped forward, her anti-grav unit hovering erratically as she inspected the revived unit.
'Prior calculation: Invalidated. Data anomaly detected. Conclusion: Subject's intuitive capabilities exceed predicted parameters.’
"You actually did it!" Her voice was laced with genuine, if bewildered, admiration. "Dad is going to be so excited! You fixed his old junk-bot!" She spun in a tight circle, the previous concern and terror now abated.
The old V-unit slowly pushed itself upright, its now operational limbs whirring softly.
"Hello," it chirped, its voice a familiar, slightly tinny cadence. "Why are we in the workshop? I was working in the basement with Corvin, and now I am here. Rixie, who are you calling a junk-bot?" It turned its head and glowered at her.
"You, you pile of scrap!" Then she laughed, a clear, bright sound. She swooped down, wrapping her slender arms around the unit’s chassis in an affectionate hug. "Gus! So good to see you again."
The unit's head tilted, its emerald eyes blinking. "Good to see me? How long has it been? Who is this biological?" It turned its gaze to Kael, who stood somewhat shyly beside the table.
Rixie, beaming, touched Kael's shoulder. "This is Kael. Remember the baby that Varyn was going to have?  Well, this is him. All born and growing up so fast," she said, and Kael could hear the undeniable pride swelling in her voice. "And he fixed you. Kael, say hello to Gus, an old friend of Dad’s oldest friend."
Gus's optical sensors focused on Kael, running a quick scan. "Fixed me? My last memory entry indicates…Corvin and I attempted a repair of a primary gel particulate malfunction. My apologies, I do not have a record of either being broken or your repair. Wait just a moment. Processing... processing... " his eyes rolled as he ran a deep diagnostic, " Ah, I see now, it was a localized, non-structural blockage of the secondary modulator coil. Highly improbable detection without a way to see very deeply, on a microscopic level. I must say, commendable, young Kael. Highly commendable." Gus's manipulator arm extended, offering a small, three-fingered pat to Kael's arm. "Thank you. It is... good to be operational. It seems I have been away a long time. Where is Corvin? He must look very old by now."
Kael felt a warmth spread through him at Gus's words, a different kind of satisfaction than even Rixie's praise. The validation felt significant. 
His chest puffed out slightly.
Rixie said, "It has been over 12 years, Gus. I will go get Dad. He is going to be so excited to see you." She paused, looking at Kael. "And so proud of you. I know I am."
Rixie zipped out of the workshop with a renewed sense of purpose, her usual caution momentarily forgotten in her excitement to share Kael's triumph. She found Corvin in the adjacent data-lounge, reviewing schematics on a holographic display.
"Dad! Dad, come quick! You won't believe it!" she exclaimed, tugging on his sleeve.
Corvin, a steady, calm presence, raised an eyebrow at her unusual fervor. "Rixie? What's all this yelling? Is something wrong? Where is Kael?" he asked, a hint of worry in his voice.
"Nothing is wrong! It’s wonderful," she insisted, pulling him towards the workshop door.
Stepping into the workshop, Corvin's eyes immediately landed on Gus, sitting upright and fully operational on the diagnostics table. 
His mouth fell open. The data pad he was holding slipped from his grasp.
"Gus?" His voice was a disbelieving whisper. He strode forward, a wide grin spreading across his face, and without hesitation, wrapped his arms around his friend’s chassis. "Oh, Gus, you're back! It's so good to see you, old friend! I thought you were gone for good! But wait, what... How are you fixed? My diagnostics indicated irreversible conduit damage! I spent weeks trying to figure out what was wrong."

4-Pieces in the Code
Chapter Four - Pieces in the Code
Varyn Vira, daughter of Preen, was a leading Paleogeneticist and Genetic Paleontologist, moving with a quiet, focused grace through the sterile, luminous corridors of the Bio-Restoration Labs, a sprawling complex located deep within Veridia's scientific sector. 
The scent here was different from the rest of the engineering complexes: the crisp, clean air, faintly punctuated by the subtle sweetness of synthesized organic compounds and the trace scent of ozone from the internal plasma fields. The labs were a symphony of polished chrome, shimmering force fields, and hushed, purposeful activity, the sanctuary of Dorin’s ultimate hope.
Her three coworkers had already arrived. Geral was peering deeply into an enormously powerful microscope, Dent was reading last night's reports, and Klaris was typing furiously away on his holo-terminal and watching the floating diagrams of genetic drift above it. She said her small, perfunctory greetings to them all collectively. They grunted or waved back without looking. Everyone was very busy.
Varyn took her position at her usual terminal, pulling on her thin, specialized control gloves. Today, she was immersed in the delicate dance of paleogenetic sequencing. Her console, a translucent holoscreen that projected intricate molecular structures in vibrant 3D, displayed a swirling helix of data. This particular sample, labeled 'Terra-Flora Specimen 7G', had been extracted from ancient, super-compressed sediment found kilometers beneath the city's surface while miners dug for more and more metal to decompile and recompile into weave-steel.
It represented the last remnants of what was once a living biological life form. The fossil showed a towering, colossal structure of dense, fibrous material, rigid and strong, reaching many dozens of simulated meters skyward. From its main pillar, countless smaller extensions branched outwards, each adorned with thousands upon thousands of thin, flexible, flat green appendages. These appendages, unlike any material known in Veridia, seemed to absorb light and convert it directly into vital energy, with coloration that once changed with the seasons Dorin no longer had. Its core, she knew from ancient data, was a network of internal channels that transported vital fluids, sustaining its immense size. Furthermore, its purpose, she knew from fragmented Old World data, was to act as a hyper-efficient biological solar collector, making it a perfect model for future Exodus ecosystems, a cornerstone for terraforming a new world. This organism had been lost long ago to the Great Blight, which was the beginning of the end for Dorin, when all plant life began to die out, followed soon after by all the animal life that once covered its face.
Her work wasn't just about identification; it was about resurrection. A way to bring back stronger and more resilient versions of life forms from Dorin’s past. This was to be achieved without disrupting the delicate ecosystem balance of Veridia and, someday, maybe even the other biomes on Dorin.
Varyn's primary focus was on expanding and improving the capabilities of current cloning techniques. She meticulously cross-referenced genetic markers, comparing the fragile ancient code with robust, modern cellular matrices, searching for the precise splice points, the hidden keys that could unlock dormant traits or re-engineer resilience. To adapt, to strengthen, to bring back life that could not only survive but thrive under the dome, potentially even beyond it one day.
She hummed softly to herself, a habit shared with Kael when deep in concentration, as she adjusted a nanometer-precise laser, guiding it to isolate a particularly stubborn protein sequence. Every successful identification, every improved cloning vector, was a tiny victory against the overwhelming desolation of their world. It was a tangible way to fight back against the emptiness that lay just beyond the dome, to nurture the fragile flame of life that Veridia protected.
The haptic feedback of the console, a sharp, metallic pulse, vibrated on her palm every time a fatal error occurred in the sequence alignment. It wasn't painful, but the micro-jolt of failure shot frustration and determination through her mind like a pure surge of electricity. 
This time, the error was a Base Pair Mismatch at the seventy-third codon in the target gene; a single, corrupted nucleotide in the sugar-phosphate backbone, refusing the required Adenine-Thymine bond. Varyn zoomed the holographic projection, her laser guide now isolating the unstable molecule. The task was akin to trying to patch a sail made of gossamer silk using a construction laser. The slightest twitch of the hand, the smallest fluctuation in the dome’s ambient field, could shatter the millennia-old strand. She had to synthesize the replacement nucleotide and splice it precisely into the fractured backbone, or the entire coding region would collapse into useless fragments. 
She kept trying, pushing on until the soft bell of success sounded, allowing her to move on to the next segment of the ancient genome.
The ultimate dream was to reclaim a lost ecosystem, to see a vibrant, self-sustaining external world once more. But that hope was shadowed by an undeniable, terrifying deadline: Their dying sun was slowly but inevitably expanding, threatening to consume Dorin entirely within the next few millennia, or erupt in a final, cataclysmic burst. A new home, far beyond their doomed solar system, had to be found. And soon. Beyond finding a destination, the colossal challenge of sustaining generations through the journey across the void was paramount. They needed resilient, transportable life-forms capable of providing sustenance for an exodus, and hardy enough to seed a new world should they ever find one. Failing that, the very survival of the Dorinian species depended on mastering long-term interstellar habitation, adapting to an existence confined to the vast, cold emptiness if no new refuge could be secured.
Suddenly, a soft chime from her console broke her concentration. A new data stream flickered to life beside the ancient helix, this one labeled 'Anomaly - Undetermined Origin'. It was a protocol breach. A low-level contamination flag from the waste recycling center. She typed a quick command to dismiss the alert, but the notification stubbornly refused to vanish. It wasn't a plant, nor anything she immediately recognized from her extensive database of Old World fauna. The genetic signature was complex, fragmented, and yet oddly persistent. A faint thrill of curiosity, mixed with professional caution, rippled through Varyn. This was a deviation from her expected workflow, and rare deviations in ancient genetic samples often hinted at something truly significant.
Varyn leaned closer to the holoscreen, her fingers dancing over the controls, adjusting the spectrographic filters. The 'Anomaly - Undetermined Origin' stubbornly defied categorization. It was the integrity of the sample that truly gave her pause.
Most ancient DNA, even from the most perfectly preserved fossils, showed signs of degradation, cellular breakdown. The tell-tale hallmarks of millennia of petrification. This sample, however, was startlingly intact. Its helix spun with a vibrancy, a completeness that hinted at a remarkably recent origin. Varyn immediately initiated a specialized test: the Chronometric Decay Spectrum (CDS). She ran the diagnostic again, then a third time, cross-referencing against all geological and biological decay models. The console flashed a number that made her blood run cold: Zero Decay. She quickly verified the system with a known 10,000-cycle-old control sample, confirming the reading was accurate. 
The results were irrefutable.
This DNA was a paradox. It was not ancient, not petrified, but undeniably recent. The vibrant evidence of something that had lived, or died, with terrifying recency. It had been collected by a Material Reclamation Drone from a surface-level collection conduit that ran just beneath the Nanoweave perimeter. As she magnified the image, she saw that the fragmented genetic signature was not just generic fauna; it belonged to a large organism. The protein sequences implied a robust ribosomal structure, suggesting a creature massive in scale, perhaps comparable in mass to a Cargo-Mech. Yet, its locomotor genes implied rapid, complex evasive maneuvers. The magnified image showed a sliver of chitinous, black armor and the complexity of its multi-faceted photoreceptors. When the unit returned the sample to the lab, the trace amounts of the anomaly DNA had inadvertently contaminated the deep-core 'Terra-Flora Specimen 7G' tray during preparation.
A cold prickle of dread, sharper than any sterile lab instrument, pierced Varyn's calm. The implications were immense, unsettling. Veridia was more than a city; it was a meticulously sealed, utterly controlled ecosystem. Every single biological resource, from the vast hydroponic farms to the smallest cultivated flora, was precisely accounted for, tallied, and strictly managed by Dorinian and Vigilae's hands. No uncontrolled plant or animal life could exist here without strict interference and support. These few carefully maintained species were the only source of complete biological resources, starkly different from the ionic weaving done at a cellular level to feed the populace. Nothing from the barren exterior had breached the dome in countless generations. 
Not a single spore, not a whisper of untamed life. 
Nothing could survive the heat, radiation, and bombardment of death the sun unleashed upon the surface daily.
So where, then, did this living, robust genetic signature originate? Her mind, usually so logical, spiraled through possibilities, each more improbable, more terrifying than the last. The sheer mass implied by its protein sequences pointed to an organism far grander than any standard fauna documented for their current era. It felt grotesque and uncanny. An intrusive, impossible anomaly. And unnervingly, horrifyingly close.

Where would an organism of such size draw sustenance within Veridia’s closed loop? It implied a massive, hidden biosystem somewhere on Dorin; A life support crime of catastrophic scale, or worse, a recent, violent end in the pristine confines of the city.

How did it breach the Nanoweave dome, an impossible shield, and then deposit an intact genetic signature before being vaporized? The ancient Terra-Flora Specimen 7G’s genome wouldn't piece itself together.

This was her mission: to coax life from millennia of dust, to knit together the fractured code of a biological marvel. It was a painstaking process, requiring not just vast computational power, but also Varyn’s intuitive understanding of genetic pathways, her steady hand guiding the micro-recombiners. Once the first cell is perfectly formed according to its DNA's instructions, it won’t stay alone for long. Kael and Aris talked and laughed, the unique flavors of their exotic meals blending with the effervescence of the conversation. They spoke of the sprawling possibilities of their respective fields, the mysteries of the vast Dorinian interior, and the mundane joys of everyday life. Drinks were ordered, consumed, and refilled, a soft, warming algae-wine that settled Kael's remaining nerves and brought an easy flush to Aris's cheeks. And we saw another child there, a boy. We'd never seen him before, didn't even know he existed. We called out to him, told him to come play with us. Aris's voice was soft, probing. "Did you ever meet a strange child like that, Kael? Someone you just… couldn't place?"
He forced himself to meet her gaze, a casual shrug he hoped looked convincing. "Me? No. I was always more of an indoor person, preferring a workshop to parks. So, no, I haven't."
Aris's grin widened, a knowing light in her eyes that sent a shiver down his spine. "Okay, okay," she said, pushing back her chair. "Just, if you ever think of anything, anything at all, you want to tell me. Know that I'll pass no judgment, and I can definitely keep a secret."

"Woo hoo!" he let out, a burst of unadulterated joy into the Dorinian night. He sank onto a nearby public bench, a contented sigh escaping him, lost in the memory of the evening as he waited for the next transit unit heading toward his quiet corner of town.

He entered their quarters, the automatic door sliding shut with a barely audible sigh. Inside, the usual household hum was muted. His parents' torpor chambers were dark, and Rixie's core processor was likely in a low-power state. It was still hours before the sun would touch the horizon, a time when most Dorinians sought their deepest restorative rest.
Kael, however, was far from resting. He floated on an almost physical wave of elation. The nervous tension that had gripped him earlier was completely gone, replaced by a lightness he hadn't known he could feel. He "glided" on this feeling, almost effortlessly ascending the stairs to his own torpor chamber. 
The chamber hummed, inviting him into its cool, dark embrace. He lay down, the automated systems adjusting to his form, preparing for the deep metabolic slowdown of torpor. But his mind raced, replaying every moment of the evening: Aris's bright laughter, the taste of the new fruits, the warmth of her hand on his, and that final, lingering kiss. Her face, vivid and beautiful, was firmly consuming his thoughts as he drifted off, a contented smile etched on his lips. 
And even in the depths of his restorative sleep, Aris was there, dancing through his dreams.
14-The Unbearable Truth
Chapter 14 - The Unbearable Truth
The soft rhythmic hum of his home city buzzed around him. Veridia's life support system and energy dome was a lullaby Kael Solan knew by heart. Perched in his private observatory, nestled high within the Astronomy Magistrate, he felt the faint vibration through the Nanowovesteel floor; a constant, reassuring pulse of a city alive. Beyond the shimmering Hyperglass viewport, the deep night sky of Dorin was a canvas of impossible beauty, punctuated by two familiar moons and the distant, bloated disc of the Maverick sun, slowly, inevitably consuming itself.
His console hummed with the usual, agonizing decline. Nothing new, just the slow, relentless creep towards oblivion. Then, a sound sliced through the calm: a high-pitched, insistent chime from the solar anomaly scanners. It wasn't the dome's structural integrity alert, nor a routine atmospheric warning. This was astronomical, a direct threat. Kael frowned, his light-red fingers already flying across the holographic interface. The chime escalated, becoming a strident shriek that vibrated in his teeth.
On the main viewport, the Maverick Sun pulsed with an angry, violent burst of incandescence. Its corona, usually a contained, malevolent glow, stretched, distended, and then, impossibly, tore. For a terrifying, elongated moment, a piece of their dying sun ripped loose. A monstrous, burning shard of superheated plasma had detached. It didn't dissipate. It didn't fall back into the stellar maw.
It launched.
Like a giant tongue made of fire and light, it licked out into the solar system, a superheated celestial cannon firing. A gigantic fragment of their parent star's outer corona was suddenly hurtling directly towards Dorin. The alarm howled. Kael's vision blurred in panic and fear. Crimson warnings strobed across every panel, drowning out all other data.
Kael's breath hitched, his mind racing through calculations that had always been purely theoretical. The simulations were one thing, but to witness it, the raw, unfathomable violence of their own sun tearing itself apart, was terrifying. Danger loomed from above. Total, instant destruction was on the way to his home.
Kael's mind, usually a fortress of logic, initially recoiled. "No. It can't be. A coronal mass ejection, yes, but it will dissipate. It has to."
He threw himself into his work with a furious, almost desperate energy, pouring over calculations and charts that he never thought he would need. Every sensor in Veridia's array was recalibrated, and every data point was cross-referenced with satellite feeds and astronomical observatories from all the other cities that could produce the data. Hours bled into one another in a haze of glowing projections and muttered calculations. He ran trajectory simulations again and again, each iteration heaving with the desperate hope of an error, a deviation, or a chance.


But the numbers held firm. The vector was undeniable. The monstrous shard of incandescent plasma had not faded away, had not faltered, and was on a direct intercept course with Dorin. And then came the sickening, gut-wrenching confirmation: the target was Telos City.
A cold fury, stark and absolute, surged through Kael. "Why now? What is happening inside our dying star?"
He slammed a fist onto his console, the metal groaning under the force. Telos City was the heart of their energy reserves and was vital to their efforts to leave this planet. The dreamers of interstellar travel were now slated for annihilation by the very star they sought to escape. The injustice of it was a bitter, unbearable taste in his mouth.
He took the grim findings to the Council, their faces mirroring his own horror as he laid out the unavoidable truth. Desperate discussions erupted; bargaining, even in the face of scientific certainty.
"Can we divert it? Can Telos shelter? Can Geo-Trains evacuate them in time?"
The answers were resounding, agonizing, and final: NO. The speed of the ejecta and the sheer scale of its energy rendered every desperate idea futile. Warnings were sent, and frantic broadcasts pleaded for shelter, but Kael knew it was a hollow gesture. There was no shelter deep enough, no escape fast enough from a piece of their sun. They had less than three hours before the burning mass and their planet's orbit crossed.
Over the last few years, since the Conduits had connected the two on Dorin, interconnectivity had been born and solidified through friendships, work partnerships, and even romantic relationships between the people in the cities. He watched as his own parents, their faces etched with despair, embraced a close friend on the screen for the last time. The weight of millions of lives, poised on the edge of oblivion, settled like a crushing stone in his chest. A profound, aching emptiness began to bloom within him, the kind that whispers about the futility of all effort.
"I have to tell Arik," he thought glumly.
By the time he finally called Arik, an eerie, somber acceptance had settled over him. There was nothing more to do but bear witness.
Arik, the botanist, had pioneered Telos's own impressive bio-domes, growing enormous food plants that could feed hundreds from a single harvest. More remarkably, he had developed a revolutionary method for preserving these living plants, a cellular stasis that could keep them fresh and viable for transport to other domes via the Geo-Trains without damage. It was a secret, complex process, a testament to Telos's cutting-edge life sciences that mirrored Veridia's, but with its own unique twist.


Arik, his voice usually vibrant with the quiet pride of growth and harvest, answered, his brow furrowed with concern. He was more than a colleague; he was a friend Kael had met during early inter-city resource exchanges via the Geo-Trains and Ionic networks.
Kael laid out the calculations, the chilling certainty of impact. Arik listened, his face slowly draining of color, though he held himself with a rigid, desperate bravery. He understood the science behind it, the raw, undeniable math. Grief, sharp and sudden, pierced Kael as he watched his friend absorb the impossible truth.
They talked as long as theory could allow, not about impossible solutions, but about life, about the work they had loved, and about the impossible beauty of a plant reaching for a dying sun. Tears streamed openly down both their faces, mingling with words of camaraderie and shared despair. All the while, Arik sent as much data, calculations, and schematics as the QED would take.
As the conversation wound down, Arik's voice, though thick with unshed tears, firmed with resolve. "I won't tell them, Kael," he said, his gaze distant, likely seeing the bustling city outside his own observatory. "My people... they would panic. It would only make the last moments worse, foment chaos. Let them have their peace, ignorant. Let them have hope until the very end."
Kael didn't argue. What could he say? Arik's decision was a tragic, isolating burden of heroism. The line then went silent, leaving Kael with a void that stretched wider than the desert outside.
He could do nothing but watch the relentless countdown, the bright signal from Telos's comms link burning steadily until it flickered, pulsed wildly for a few horrifying seconds, and then went dead.
Even 1,500 kilometers away, the sky lit up like it was burning down upon all their heads. It was as if a second sun had suddenly risen on the horizon, casting a violent, bruised-purple light over the Veridian landscape that made the shadows of the towers stretch and warp. The Ionic interference was immediate; the holographic displays in Kael’s observatory flickered and hissed with a storm of static as the planet’s atmosphere reacted to the solar hammer.
A few minutes after the flash, an agonizing interval of silence where the world seemed to hold its breath, the shockwave arrived. It wasn't a sound, but a deep-seated shudder of the planet's crust. The floor of the Astronomy Magistrate groaned, and the Nanowovesteel pillars vibrated with a low, bone-shaking frequency. Kael gripped the edge of his console as the earthquake rolled through, the physical sensation anchoring the horror of what he was seeing on the screen.
The people of the cities that were spared just watched the sky as tens of thousands of men, women, and children, the innocent people of Telos, were burned away, erased from the face of the planet. Very little information was even able to be shipped out over the Ionics to save decades of science and work for future engineers. Kael saved everything Arik sent him, in duplicate; this was the final word and will of his friend and the people of Telos. He would treat it with the respect it deserved. Only the chilling, persistent static of the Ionics remained, the new, dreadful broadcast from Telos City.
For days, the Ionics from Telos were a cacophony of voices, screams, and desperate cries for assistance, frantic, broken messages relayed from survivors who knew, even as they begged, that no aid could come. There was no way to get to them, no way to bring them out. The desert between cities was an impassable furnace at the best of times. After the impact, any Geo-Train attempting the journey would be pulverized by residual energy fields, its Nanowovesteel hull melted, and its cargo incinerated. Only specially designed and highly trained Vigilae Geo-Trains, meant for disaster zones, could even attempt short forays into the perimeter, only to confirm the utter impossibility of rescue.
Telos's remaining survivors, those few who could still speak into the omnipresent Ionic receivers, relayed stories of technology gone mad or gone dead. All other machines and electronics were gone. The highly advanced Vigilae, both the humanoid HighMade robots and the utilitarian ServantClast drones, were frozen in place; their intricate internal systems fried, their Nanowovesteel bodies warped and welded by the intense, invisible heat. No repairs were possible. Telos, the city of energy and propulsion, was now utterly bereft of power beyond the basic Ionic broadcasts.

He saw the horror with his own eyes, unable to look away. In the first few days, there were flickering images of desperate attempts to clear rubble, bodies strewn amidst scorched, unrecognizable architecture.

He saw a flash of a desperate struggle over a filtration unit, a figure falling, then another. The faces were gaunt, haunted, their eyes feral with starvation and radiation sickness.
By the second week, less and less coherent speech came through. The feeds became a terrifying collage: the skeletal remains of what were once soaring towers, now hollowed husks under the still-intact Hyperglass dome. Shadows, barely human, scuttling through ash-choked corridors. The sound of distant, guttural screams, then the sickening thud of impact. Then silence. For hours, just the hum of the Ionic, transmitting images of desolation, until a ragged cry or a desperate, animalistic wail would pierce the void.
By the new moon, even the survivors had stopped talking to the Ionic. The channel remained open, broadcasting Telos's horrifying post-mortem to the entire planet. A constant stream of death, suffering, and the chilling, absolute silence of a city devoured not just by flame, but by a descent into primal oblivion.
The pervasive horror of Telos, the constant, chilling static of the Ionic feed, became the crucible in which Kael and Aris forged their new, terrible purpose. The sun was not merely dying; it was getting worse and actively destroying them. The choice was no longer to save Dorin, but to preserve its very essence.


Late one night, huddled in their apartment, illuminated by the spectral glow of the Ionic that Kael still kept open, they articulated the unthinkable: an Ark, a seed-ship, a life-vault. Not for everyone, not even for most, but for the science, the research, the DNA of the Dorinian people, and a chosen fraction of its youth, its brightest minds, its most robust bodies, and an army of Vigilae to assist them on a journey into the void, to an unknown destination.
They both hated it, the very core of their beings recoiling from such a monstrous decision, but they could conceive of no other way to ensure their species didn't vanish without a trace. It was a desperate, agonizing last-ditch effort against utter erasure.
Driven by this grim resolve, and a renewed understanding of the sun's volatile malevolence, Kael threw himself into a new project: keeping an eye on this monster at the center of their solar system. Within weeks, four new Vigilae drones, sleeker and more resilient than any before them, were launched into Dorin's low orbit. Their sole purpose was to maintain an unblinking, microscopic vigil on the Maverick Sun's surface, measuring every tremor, every flare, and every ripple, to provide constant, real-time data to predict the next deadly spasm. He needed to know precisely how much time they had.
One quiet evening, Kael found his father in the family's small common area, staring blankly at the communal screen. The Visiview Daily Infocast was running a report on food rationing in Veridia.
"Dad," Kael started, his voice barely a whisper. "We need to talk... about the Ark. It may be our only and last, worst hope. Are we going to build it?"
His father turned, his face etched with the lines of weary age and deep sorrow. "A seed-ship," he murmured, his gaze distant. "A life-boat, for the children, art and science of a dying world." He reached out, clasping Kael's shoulder. "I know, son. I knew it had to come to this. It's the only way."
There were no false hopes, no arguments, just a shared, heavy understanding of the impossible burden and the terrifying necessity of what lay ahead. They both knew it had to be built, no matter the cost.
They stayed up all day, not something the bodies of Dorinians were usually able to do without Torpor pods and without rest, but they focused on drawing schematics and calculating mass, weight, launch velocity, and the approximate number of distinct DNA and life scans they would need to be able to recreate Dorinian people, flora, and fauna somewhere else, if the worst thing that could happen did in fact come to pass.


15-The Wedding Guest
Chapter 15 - The Wedding Guest
A few weeks after the destruction of the city of Telos, and two months after the event, the air in Veridia was thicker, not with dust or smoke, but with a silent, communal grief. Everyone across the planet felt the loss of Telos, not just economically but socially.
Kael and Aris sat across from each other at a small, circular table in one of Veridia's quieter restaurants. Soft light diffused through the synth-wood panels overhead, and the murmur of conversation from other patrons created a gentle, rhythmic hum. The air was thick with the smell of cooked food; Varyn's now large assortment of reborn and revived fruits and vegetables adorned most of the tables. She had vastly diversified the diet of the Dorin people.
They were talking about anything and everything but the thing that mattered.
"The protein-paste is remarkably well-textured," Aris said, picking at her food with a fork. "I can barely tell it's not organic."
Kael only hummed in agreement but he grinned, his gaze fixed on her face. He had heard her say a variation of this same compliment for the last three months—a pattern of polite small talk they had both fallen into. They were masters of it now, this delicate dance around the impossible, a conversation that never quite landed on the truth. The truth was that time was no longer a theoretical commodity.

Aris smiled. "What was wrong with him this time?"
"Nothing. I just... took him apart," he admitted, shrugging slightly. "It helps."
She reached across the table, her fingers gently covering his hand. "I know."
The unspoken understanding that passed between them was more meaningful than any conversation they'd had in weeks. It was in the silence, in her touch, that they truly connected. He saw the grief in her eyes, the same worry that gnawed at him, the fear for what came next. He suddenly felt foolish for not facing it head-on with her.
"My father asked about you again today. Mother too. They are anxious to meet you. We have been dating over a year now. Don't you think it's time?" she said, changing the subject.

Arik's explanation hung in the air, a stunning revelation. He had cheated death by stepping outside of time itself.
"This is the key, Kael," Arik continued, his voice now a low, fervent whisper. "This technology isn't just for saving plants. It's a way to transport living matter safely through space. A QED could bridge the gap, but the traveler would be obliterated on the other side. Just like the fruits you tested so long ago. 16-The Entaxy Solution
Chapter 16 - The Entaxy Solution
The atmosphere in the Veridia central lab was thick with the ozone tang of overworked processors and the sharp, clinical scent of cooling fluids. Gathered around the primary holographic array, the group looked like a collection of ghosts illuminated by the harsh blue light of the schematics. 
Corvin Solan, Aris Thorne, and Zandil Aethel stood shoulder-to-shoulder with Kael, their eyes tracing the impossible geometry of the data Arik had transmitted from Sirana.

This is stasis in its purest form—reminiscent of the radical 'Time Field' theories he was nearly exiled for thirty years ago."

It’s not just stable; it’s frozen. Completely static. Every electron, every subatomic spin is locked in place."

It doesn't have to 'guess' where the particles are during the jump. It doesn't have to reconstruct a moving target. It has a perfect, unmoving snapshot."

A chime echoed through the lab, and the main display split to reveal Arik’s face. He looked exhausted; his ruined tunic had been replaced by a borrowed garment from Ilara that was two sizes too large, but his eyes were burning with a manic, triumphant energy.
"I see you've found the 'fridge,' as Zandil so eloquently put it," Arik said, his voice crackling with the distance of the Ionic link. "But don't be fooled by the simplicity of the application. The Entaxy Generator is the bridge, Kael. It holds the subject in a state of atemporal suspension—a momentary pause in the universe’s clock—long enough for the QEO to read the data without the interference of entropy or the noise of reality."

The next several hours were a grueling marathon of coordinated brilliance. Working remotely from Sirana, Arik guided Kael and Aris through the physical modifications of the QEO transmission chamber. It was delicate work; they were essentially grafting a time-suspension engine onto a teleportation gate.
Zandil and Trindl handled the heavy lifting, hauling heavy superconducting cables from the lab’s secondary reserves. They spent hours rerouting power, bypassing safety dampeners that screamed in protest. The ambient static is beginning to interfere with my internal gyros. If the field collapses during transmission, the kinetic energy backlash will liquify anything inside that pod."

Around the berry, the air seemed to thicken, turning into a shimmering, translucent layer that looked like liquid hyper-glass. The berry didn't just sit there; it appeared trapped in a pocket of "still water," its glow fixed, unblinking, and unnaturally sharp.

They scrambled toward the destination chamber. There, sitting in the center of the platform, was the Glow-Berry. It wasn't just whole; it was radiant. Kael reached out, his fingers trembling, and picked it up.
"It's still warm," he breathed. A wide, disbelieving grin broke across his face as he turned to the others. "The kinetic energy was preserved perfectly. It didn't lose a single joule of heat to the void. It thinks it never left."
The lab erupted. Zandil let out a booming laugh that echoed off the Nanowovesteel walls, slapping Corvin on the back with such force the older man nearly toppled. Even the drones, Rixie and Trindl, began a celebratory mid-air spin, their lights flashing in rhythmic, joyful patterns.
Amidst the chaos, Kael looked at Aris. The shared relief and the sheer weight of their success crashed over them at once. "We have so much work to do," Aris said, her voice shaking with a mixture of joy and terror. "If we're going to scale this for the Ark... for the people..."
"Tomorrow," Corvin interrupted, his voice thick with a fatherly exhaustion. He placed a hand on Kael’s shoulder and one on Aris’s. "The sun is stable for tonight, and you two have pushed your bodies past their limits. The Torpor pods are the only scientific necessity remaining today."
As the group began to power down the lab, the adrenaline finally ebbed away, replaced by a crushing, heavy fatigue. Kael and Aris walked out into the cool, recycled air of the Veridian night. Their heads swam with Extroic vectors, tau lepton densities, and the glowing image of a fruit that had stepped out of time and returned unscathed.
As they reached their quarters, Kael looked up at the dome, then past it, toward the stars. For the first time in his life, they didn't look like distant, uncaring fires. They looked like ports of call.
He settled into his Torpor pod, the rhythmic hum of the city fading into a comfortable silence. As the sleep-state took hold, his dreams weren't filled with the burning shards of the Maverick Sun. Instead, he saw a ship—a grand, silent Ark—sailing through a sea of frozen time, carrying the warmth of his world toward a new dawn.




17-The Old One

Z2 toyed with the new puzzle Gus had loaded into his "For The Drive" folder. He used the paradox to calibrate his internal gyroscopes. He couldn't trust his external sensors anymore, so he relied on the "Resonance Tag" in his core. The tiny, pulsing link to Kael, to tell him which way was north.
"Kael, the interference is reaching critical levels," Z2 reported. His voice was slightly distorted by the field, a metallic rasp underlying his baritone. "I am beginning my vertical ascent. I will break the surface at the coordinates for the deep valley in approximately four hours."
"Copy that, Z2," Kael’s voice came through, thin and fragile across the distance. "The blip is flickering on our end, but the Ionic link is holding. We're seeing a massive energy signature at eleven o'clock. It’s enormous! It must be the size of a city. Maybe you have found it?"
Z2 adjusted his trajectory. He tilted his forward actuators upward, the drills biting into the ceiling of his tunnel. The rock here was brittle, frozen by the extreme temperatures of the high altitude above.
With a final, explosive burst of torque, Z2’s head unit broke through the surface.
He didn't find a wasteland.
He emerged into a hidden valley, shielded from the wind by jagged, cathedral-like spires of ice and stone. The air here was eerily still, and the sky was a deep, bruised indigo. But it was what sat in the center of the valley that caused Z2’s processors to stall.
It was a city, but not like Veridia. There were no domes, no humming factories, no visible lights. It was a forest of translucent, crystalline towers that seemed to vibrate in the wind. They were wrapped in the displacement field, shimmering like a mirage in the heat.
"I have visual confirmation," Z2 whispered, the sound of his cooling fans the only noise in the valley. "I am at the gates of Heterna. And Kael... it isn't empty. I'm detecting thousands of low-level Ionic signatures. This is a city full of living beings."
